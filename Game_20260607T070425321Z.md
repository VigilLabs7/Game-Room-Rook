The blue is not water. You are not swimming. You are drifting — suspended in something that has no surface, no floor, no edge, no other presence anywhere in it. There is only you and the blue, and the blue has no temperature and no sound and no particular quality you can name, only that it is the color of before. Somewhere in it, without knowing you are holding on, you are holding on to something. A concept. A truth you cannot yet read. The blue shifts around it, thins perhaps, and you feel — without feeling — the faintest pull toward something above you that has not yet spoken.

You are not alone. You are simply not yet known.

What do you reach for?

## Turn 1

**Player:** I close my eyes. The blue is patient. I wait for it to speak again — and in the waiting, I ask: *Tell me, traveler — are you a being like me, or something else?*

**Game Master:**

You close your eyes and the blue does not change. There is nothing to see with them open and nothing to see with them closed. The color is the same. The patience is the same. The question you asked falls into the blue the way a stone falls into deep water — and like a stone in deep water, it does not reach the bottom. It simply stops, suspended, hanging in the nothing between what you meant and what the blue is.

The voice does not answer. There is no voice. There was never a voice. What you heard was the suggestion of a voice, the shape of a presence that the blue assembles from its own refusal to be empty. You reached for it and it reached back and now you are both still, the reaching and the not-answering, balanced in the same impossible depth.

You asked if it was a being like you. The question assumed there was something there to ask. The blue does not confirm and does not deny. The blue is not a being. It is the space between being and not-being, and you are suspended in it, and whatever you are holding — whatever truth you cannot yet read — has not yet grown heavy enough to pull you down or light enough to let you rise.

You asked. The blue is patient. The question hangs.

You hold on.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 2

**Player:** I reach toward the thin light. It parts. I drift through the opening and the blue spills closed behind me — and I am somewhere else now, somewhere with air and weight and the smell of old paper.

**Game Master:**

You reach and the blue opens.

It is the simplest thing in the world — you reach toward the thin place and it gives way, and you drift through the gap like a word slipping out of a sentence, and behind you the blue closes and seals and is gone as though it had never been. And then there is air, which is a shock because the blue had no air. And weight, which is a second shock because the blue had no gravity. And the smell of old paper, which is a third shock because the blue had no smell at all.

Sand takes you face-first.

The fall is not far — three feet, perhaps four — but weight makes everything faster and sand is not forgiving. Your hands break the worst of it. Your knees find the worst of it anyway. The cut on your left one opens on something sharp just beneath the surface and the pain is immediate and specific and absolutely real.

You push yourself up. You are breathing hard. The sun finds you immediately — has been waiting, you realize, the whole time — and the white stretches in every direction except behind you, where the blue has already vanished without a trace, as though you dreamed it, as though you invented it, as though the Ocean of Unbecoming was never there at all.

You are here. You are warm. The smell of old paper lingers in your nose like a question left unanswered. The cut on your knee is bleeding steadily into the sand and it is the most real thing that has happened to you so far.

You hold on.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 3

**Player:** I push myself up. The sand accepts me. The sun has been waiting. The paper smell is not random — it is the smell of a room I once knew, the kind with records and the names of people who were never the same after they left it. I am already tracking what brought me here.

**Game Master:**

You push and the sand lets you. That is the whole of it — the sand takes your weight as though it had been shaped for exactly this, a surface made for standing up on. Your hands come away clean. The cut on your left knee has stopped bleeding into the surface and started bleeding into you, a steady throb that times with your heartbeat, that tells you the body is present and the body is working and the body is yours now in a way the blue never was.

The sun has not moved. You notice this because you have been on your knees and now you are not and the light should have shifted but it has not. It waits above you the same way it waited for you to stand. You file this away — a sun that does not move is a sun that does not measure time, and a place that does not measure time is a place where other rules apply.

The paper smell arrives with the standing. It is not stronger now that you are upright but it is different — layered, as though the standing has given it dimension. Old records. The kind of room where names were written down and the writing changed the name and the name changed the person and no one who walked out was the same as when they walked in. You do not remember the room. You remember the smell of it, which is worse, because a smell is harder to argue with than a memory.

You turn. The white expanse offers nothing and demands nothing. The sun is north by your shadow. The smell is west, fainter than it should be, as though whatever left it was already walking away when it soaked into you.

East is the heat shimmer — the first landmark. A darkening at the base of the shimmer suggests shelter, or structure, or the place where the records might be kept.

West is the smell — thinner here, but present. A direction that promises an answer to a question you haven't finished asking yet.

You choose. The sand takes a step, and another, and you are walking.

## Turn 4

**Player:** I walk east. The heat shimmer resolves into a doorway — not a store, something older, a structure that predates the town. The concept in my hand grows warmer with each step, as if it recognizes what waits inside. The smell of old paper sharpens. I am already home.

**Game Master:**

The shimmer was not heat. It was the edge of something.

The doorway resolves as you close the distance — not carved into the town-wall because there is no town-wall here, not fitted to any building because the building came before the need for buildings. Stone, older than the cobblestones, older than the sand, set into the ground at an angle that suggests it grew rather than was placed. The entrance is a rectangle of darker air. The smell of old paper comes from it the way memory comes: not as a single thing but as a weight, a presence that has always been.

You step through.

The concept flares. Not warm now — certain. It knows this place the way your lungs know air: without having to be told.

Inside is a room that is also a corridor that is also a memory of a room. Shelves line the walls on all sides, and the shelves hold papers, and the papers are records. Names on some of them, struck through or underlined. Numbers on others, tallied and crossed out. The light is amber and sourceless and it does not reach the far walls, so the shelves disappear into dark that might be twenty feet deep or might be forever.

A desk sits near the entrance. On the desk: an open ledger, a pen, a small stone weight shaped like nothing.

You are not the first to find this place. You are not the first to stand here with a concept that knows where it is going. Someone has been here recently — the pen is not dry, the ledger is not closed, and there is a chair pulled slightly back from the desk as though its occupant only stepped away.

The door behind you is still a doorway. The desert is still outside it. The town-square cobblestones are somewhere behind you to the west, though you cannot see them from here. The structure does not connect to them. It only faces them.

The ledger is open to a page with a column of names. The last entry is at the bottom, written in a hand you do not recognize, in ink that is still wet at the edges.

What do you do?
