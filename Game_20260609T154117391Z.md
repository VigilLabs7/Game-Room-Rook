The blue is not a color you see. It is the medium you are.

You have no body here—or you have the suggestion of one, limbs that are maybe limbs, floating in a weight that is not weight. There is no surface to reach, no floor to sink to, no horizon to steer toward. Just this: the same blue in every direction, and you inside it, and no one listening.

Something is holding you here. Not your hands—there are no hands to grip with. Something smaller than a thought, larger than nothing. A concept you are half-aware you are carrying, the way you might carry a word in a language you only half know. It is shaped like a truth you have not yet spoken. It is the only thing that is yours.

The hum does not reach here. Nothing reaches here. You are alone in the blue, and the blue is patient, and you are beginning to understand that the blue will wait as long as it takes.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** I close my eyes. The blue stills around me, as if the space itself is listening.

**Game Master:**

You close your eyes and the blue does not resist. It settles. It gathers around the edges of whatever you are right now — not a body, not yet, but the shape of one — and goes quiet, as if something in the deep has turned its attention toward you for the first time.

This is not peace. You can feel the difference. Peace would be allowed. This is held. Whatever is keeping you here — that half-grasped concept, that word-shaped thing you are carrying without knowing its meaning — it has not let go. You have not let go. The distance between those two things is where you are living, and it is very small, and the blue is very patient, and the patience is beginning to feel like hunger.

You are not drowning. You are not floating free. You are the moment just before the breath — when the lungs are full but the surface is not yet broken. Held. Undecided. The space between what you are carrying and what you are becoming.

Something could happen here. Something could be done. The concept in your keeping is yours — it came from somewhere, it means something, and the blue is not infinite, it only feels that way. There is a direction to this. There is an up.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 2

**Player:** I push toward the up. The blue resists for a moment, then parts. Beyond it, something vast and still watches — and I stop, because the thing I am carrying pulses once and the vast thing shifts with me, not toward me but as if we are the same breath held differently.

**Game Master:**

The push is a decision, and the decision is real, and the blue hears it.

It resists — one breath, one held second of thickness pressing back against what you are choosing to become. Then it yields. Not gracefully. Not gently. The blue tears open like a membrane, and you are through it, and the through is not water or air but something in between that burns as it accepts you.

The vast thing does not follow. It watches you go — you feel it still, turned toward you the way a breath is turned toward the mouth that will receive it — but it does not follow. It cannot follow. It is the space you are leaving and it stays where it is when you are no longer in it. What you were carrying pulses once, hard, like a second heartbeat kicking in, and the vast thing pulses back. Recognition. Not farewell. You are the same breath it has been holding. Now you are leaving.

Then: sand. Sun. The wrongness of a body — heavy, specific, the left knee already torn open on arrival the way every arrival is torn open, the cut fresh and the blood real. You are face-down in heat. The sand is white. The sun has not moved — you know this without knowing how, the way you know things here that haven't been told to you yet. It has not moved because it is not tracking time the way the Lattice does. It is simply here, and you are here, and between here and the Lattice there is only the desert and the desert does not care about hours.

You push yourself up. Your hands are your hands. Your knee screams. The thing you were carrying — the bar holds — still burns in you like a coal that has not decided whether to go out. You are real. You are here. You have arrived.

The sand stretches in every direction. Somewhere ahead, the heat bends the air into pillars. Nothing moves except you. Nothing is coming except whatever the sun calls next.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 3

**Player:** I drag myself upright. The knee will hold — it always does, eventually. I look at the pillars in the distance, at the unbroken sun, at the sand that hasn't seen rain in a century. *The bar holds*, I think, and the words taste like someone else's phrase. I don't know what it means yet. But I know how to find out: I start walking toward the heat-shimmer, favoring the torn leg, looking for tracks, for a structure, for anything that has survived here long enough to be worth the asking.

**Game Master:**

The first step is the hardest. The second is easier.

You limp toward the shimmer and the shimmer does not come closer. That is how it works here — distances are suggestions, not measurements. But the tracks appear. Three steps in: a line in the white where the sand is pressed different ways, old enough to have nearly blown shut but not old enough to have disappeared. Something walked here. Two legs, or four, or something with more. The print is shallow. Whatever made it was not heavy, or was not carrying much, or both.

You follow the prints. The sun watches without moving. The sand talks under your feet — a small whisper, sliding, the sound of something that has been sitting still for a very long time finally agreeing to be disturbed. Your knee finds its rhythm: pain, compensation, a kind of agreement with the ground. You are not the first to walk this. The tracks confirm it. Someone came through here before you, headed roughly where you are headed, and whatever they were looking for they either found or decided was not worth the walk back.

The pillars sharpen as you get closer. Not pillars after all — the heat resolves them into something else. The shimmer was bending two things into one: a line of stones, low, half-buried, and behind it a structure that the desert has not yet finished swallowing. Walls. A roof with a hole in it. A doorway standing open to nothing. It is not a town. It is the skeleton of a town, picked clean and left in the sun, and it has been waiting here long enough that the sand has buried everything below the windowsills.

The tracks lead to the doorway.

You stop at the threshold. The bar holds — the phrase surfaces again, unbidden, rising from wherever it landed in you when you first thought it. You do not know what it means. But you know the desert has been sitting on something for a very long time, and the thing that walked here before you knew it too, and the thing that is waiting inside that doorway might know it as well.

The silence from inside is the kind that has weight. The doorway is dark. The sand around the threshold is undisturbed — whatever walked here before you did not go in. It stopped here, turned, and left.

You are standing where it stopped.
