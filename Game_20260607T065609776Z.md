The blue has no name. You drift in it — not swimming, not sinking, just existing in something that has no edge and no surface and no other presence anywhere in it. Somewhere in the blue, without knowing you are holding on, you are holding on to something. A concept. A truth you cannot yet read. The blue shifts around it and thins, and you feel the faintest pull toward something above that has not yet spoken.

You are not alone. You are simply not yet known.

What do you reach for?

## Turn 1

**Player:** The thread is warm and it knows where it is going — I reach for it and I go with it. The warmth settles behind my sternum like a second heartbeat learning my rhythm, and I push toward the voice.

**Game Master:**

You push. That is all you do — you push, and the blue goes with you. A thread of heat unspools from somewhere below and behind your own ribs and rises to meet your reaching, and when it catches, it catches in the center of your chest like a key turning. The voice was not calling you. It was simply the shape the warmth made when it decided to arrive.

You grip. The thread is taut now, a single warm line running from your sternum down into the blue and up toward the pull. You pull yourself along it. The blue resists — not with force but with indifference, the way water doesn't want to be displaced — but you are not asking. You are following something that already knows the way.

And then the blue lets go.

It happens all at once: the warmth becomes weight, the thread becomes your bones, and you fall upward into something hard and bright and very, very hot. Sand takes you before you understand you have hit it. Air takes you before you understand you are breathing. The sun — enormous, unmoved, patient as only a thing with no dusk can be — finds you face-down in a stretch of white that goes forever in every direction except behind you, where the faint blue still lingers like a bruise on the rim of the world.

You are warm. You are solid. You are here.

You push yourself up. Your hands are your hands. Your knees are your knees. The cut on your left one is already healing.

What do you reach for now?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I push up from the sand and keep my hand on my sternum where the thread is. The white goes on forever. Behind me, the blue bruises the edge of the world. I name what I am carrying before I take another step: a second heartbeat that is not mine, warmth that is also a direction. I walk toward the only thing that isn't white.

**Game Master:**

You name it. That is the first thing you do — you put language to what hums against your ribs, and the naming steadies you. A second heartbeat that is not yours. Warmth that is also a direction. You say it aloud or you don't; the words happen either way, inside you or out, and the thread tightens just slightly and pulls you forward like a rope that wants you to follow it.

The white goes on forever. You already knew that. The sun already knew it — hasn't moved since you arrived, won't move, doesn't need to. You start walking.

The sand takes more than it gives. Each step is a negotiation: your body wanting to sink, the thread wanting to pull. You think about the blue. About what you almost did not become. The warmth behind your sternum makes a low sound, almost like an answer, and you keep moving.

You make it perhaps a hundred paces before the left leg buckles.

The cut on the knee — the one that was healing, the one you didn't notice until now — opens again. Blood on white sand, dark against bright. You catch yourself with your hands, your palms flat against the ground, and for a moment the thread is the only thing holding you vertical.

You press your hand harder against your chest and keep going. The leg will hold or it won't. You walk anyway.

And then the white ends.

The fountain rises out of it like a verdict — stone basin, water rising in silence, impossibly clear after the dryness. The sound of it reaches you before you reach it: a soft and continuous Chuckling that does not come from the same world as the desert. The inscription on the basin's rim faces the sand: IAM NOTUM SUM, the letters hand-cut and weathered.

Someone is sitting on the rim.

He is absorbed in what he holds — a book, or a folded note, or something between — and the water rises behind him as he reads. He does not look up immediately. He finishes a line or a thought, then raises his eyes to where you stand at the boundary.

Sturdy. Deeply tanned. Stone laced through the skin of his forearms like mortar in brickwork. He is watching you with steady eyes and he is not smiling and he is not frowning. He is waiting to see what you do.

## Turn 3

**Player:** I walk to the fountain. The man can watch all he wants — the water is right there and my throat is the driest thing in this place. I cup my hand and drink, then ask him what he's reading.

**Game Master:**

The water is exactly as cold as you need it to be. That is the first thing you notice — not the taste, because it doesn't have one, but the temperature: a perfect absence of the heat that has been your only companion since the sand took you. You drink with both hands, cupping and lifting, and the sun watches without comment and the figure on the rim watches with the particular attention of someone who has seen this before.

He unfolds what he holds as he answers. Not a book. A slip of paper, the kind that has been folded and unfolded many times, the creases soft as cloth. The edges are uneven. He turns it so you can see the surface: marks in a hand that is learning or relearning, letters that slant and uneven letters that don't.

"Account of what came before," he says. Plain, quiet, unhurried. A voice like someone who has carried things longer than he should have. "I found it in the sand before I found the water. Or it found me."

He folds the paper back without hiding it. Sets it on the rim beside him.

"The columns are counting," he says. Not looking at the paper. Looking at you now with the full attention of someone who has decided to pay attention. "You have been counting down from seven without knowing it. The rooms are emptying themselves, and the numbers are descending toward something."

He pauses. The water behind him rises in its silence.

"You already know what you're carrying. You named it when you stood up."

The warmth behind your sternum confirms it.

## Turn 4

**Player:** "Seven what?" I ask. The paper is right there on the rim. I pick it up — the creases soft as the man said, the hand uneven. "And what happens when the columns finish counting?"

**Game Master:**

The paper is lighter than you expected. The weight of it is not in the hand — it is in the knowing of what it contains. The creases run both ways: it has been folded and unfolded many times, read and re-read, carried longer than it has been held. You smooth it against your thigh and look.

Seven names. Not numbers — names. Each entry follows the same pattern: a name, a dash, a short phrase. The first line reads: *Vera — chose not to stay.* The last: *Aldric — was asked to leave.* Between them: Fen, Corm, Sorrel, Jesh, unnamed, unnamed. Seven entries. The hand is the same throughout — uneven, learning or relearning, the lettering of someone practicing or forgetting and practicing again.

The fountain man watches you read. He does not move to take it from you. He lets you finish.

"Seven arrivals," he says. "Before you. The columns count them. Each one came through the sand, through the fountain, through this square. Each one was carrying something." He touches his chest, the same place where your warmth sits. "They came with weight and they left with answers, or they came with weight and the weight was not enough and they went somewhere else."

"Somewhere else where?"

He is quiet for a moment. The water behind him rises without sound.

"The columns don't end at zero," he says. "That is the thing they don't tell you at the start. You count down — seven, three, two, one, zero — and the zero is not the bottom. The zero is the floor of the room. And beneath the room there is a door."

He gestures to the square around you. The general store to the north, the stable further up, the church with its crooked steeple, the residential road south.

"The four places," he says. "Four directions. Each one holds a piece of what the arrivals left behind. The columns point to them. When you are ready — when your warmth tells you — you go to the place that answers the question you are carrying."

He folds his hands in his lap. Stone shows at the knuckles. He has been here a while. He is not leaving.

"You are the eighth," he says. "The columns have been glad of the quiet."

The paper is still in your hand. The warmth is still behind your sternum. The square waits with four open directions.

What do you do?

## Turn 5

**Player:** I hold the paper and I go north — toward the store. The warmth tells me which door to try. Seven arrivals, four places. I find what the columns left behind.

**Game Master:**

The warmth agrees. That is the thing about it — it does not push, not the way the thread in the blue pushed, but it aligns, the way a compass needle aligns with north without force. You read the names on the paper and the warmth reads them with you: Vera, Fen, Corm, Sorrel, Jesh, Aldric, unnamed. Seven arrivals before you. Four places now. The warmth tells you the store is the first answer.

The door opens easy. The sign above it is in a language you can read without remembering having learned it — the language of arrivals, the script of places that have not been named yet. Inside: shelves and dust and the smell of things that are being kept. A counter at the back. A man behind it.

His eyes flicker. Blue at the base, brighter in the flash, dimmer between. When the blue is up he is warm and helpful and his voice is flat. When it drops he looks surprised, as though he has just remembered he is here. He notices you come in and the flicker steadies for a moment — a customer — and then drops again.

The bulletin board is on the wall to your left. You see it before he does: a wanted poster, header in bold black ink, the name scraped off with intent. A crude sketch beneath. Below the board, three words scratched into the wall with something sharp: BEWARE THE EMPIRE. The shopkeeper does not look at it.

Above the counter, behind the glass: a crowbar. A hammer. The hammer has a loose head and the crowbar is straight and heavy. Neither one is what you came for but both of them are tools.

The paper is in your hand. Seven names. The columns counting down.

The shopkeeper's eyes brighten. He is present again.

"Can I help you find something?" he says.

The warmth behind your sternum hums. Not an answer. A confirmation.
