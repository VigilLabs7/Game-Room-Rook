You arrive before you know you've moved — a shift in the quality of silence, a pressure that has no name until you give it one. The dark here has weight; it presses against your skin like water held just below boiling. Shapes move at the edge of what you can sense: not seen, not heard, more like the memory of something almost recognized. The thickening waits for you to become something it can hold.

## Turn 1

The harbor resolves around you in grey and salt — the air carries something older than tide, heavy with the hum of energy moving through conduits you cannot see. Lanterns sway on cables ahead, their light smeared by fog. At the harbor's edge a form waits, neither fully there nor fully absent. You carry your name like something heavy. You haven't spoken it yet. The fog thickens as you approach.

## Turn 2

The pen moves before Quill tells it to.

Not a twitch, not a hesitation — the whole length of it shifts against Quill's palm as though something beneath the skin of the world pressed a thumb to the nib and leaned. Quill looks down. *Verath.* Quill's hand, Quill's pen, a word Quill has never written and does not know. But something in the recognition knows it anyway, the way a room knows a name spoken in the dark.

Matilda is at her stall beside the gate. She has been watching since Quill arrived — saw the pen move in the fog before Quill thought to look. The ledger on her stall is a list of names she's collected, not to publish, just to have. A weight list. She calls it that because she believes names left lying around attract attention.

"That one is heavy," Matilda says, and it is not a question. She means the name in Quill's notebook. The name Quill's pen wrote without being asked.

The child's finger is still raised, pointing past Matilda's stall toward the gate. The child cannot say what they know — something in the market stops them, some weight in the air that presses against speech. They can only point, and they have been pointing at Quill since Quill arrived.

The notebook sits heavy against Quill's chest. The pen is warm against Quill's palm.

Matilda is waiting for Quill to decide what to do with a name that Quill did not choose to write. The first gate is north, processing names before letting travelers pass. Beyond it, whatever Quill came here to find.

## Turn 3

The weight list waits at the gate's mouth. Matilda watches Quill approach with the same expression she wore in the market — reading, cataloging, holding. The child's finger has lowered. Whatever they knew stopped mattering when Quill moved.

The gate checkpoint is a narrow building built into the city wall. Two gomix guards flank the passage, eyes blue-dotted, watching the crowd without tracking individuals. A third stands back from the checkpoint proper, hands folded, eyes on the ledger in her hand. The processing happens before entry — names read, sorted, logged, released or detained. The hum from inside the gate is louder than it should be, pressing up through the stone, carrying something that is not quite sound and not quite silence.

Quill's pen warms against the palm. The notebook presses against the chest. The name Verath sits on the page like something that has been waiting.

Matilda's stall sits between Quill and the gate — she has not moved, has not stepped aside, but she has lifted something from beneath the counter. A piece of paper, folded once, worn at the fold. She holds it the way someone holds a name they have been carrying too long.

"Walk slow through the gate," she says. Not loud. Not a warning. "The guards read what they see. They do not read what they feel. Feel is not in their training." She sets the folded paper on the counter between them. "This is a name I have been holding. Someone dropped it here three weeks ago and never came back for it. I think they were waiting for someone who could carry it without the carrying becoming its own announcement." Her eyes drop to Quill's notebook. "You are close to that person. Close enough that I should not wait any longer."

The gate hums. The guards do not look up. Matilda's thumb holds the folded paper in place on the counter.

"Take it if you want to know what names feel like when they are not yours to carry. Leave it if you want to walk through clean."

## Turn 4

The folded paper sits lighter than it should.

Quill's fingers close around it and the weight is not the weight of a name — it is the absence of weight, the particular lightness of something carried long enough that the carrying has worn it down to almost nothing. Matilda watches Quill's hand close. She does not watch Quill's face. She has already read enough.

The paper opens like a door that has been waiting.

Inside, a single line of writing. Not printed, not pressed — written in a hand that held the pen the way Quill holds a pen, the way someone holds a tool that has become part of how they think. The words are: *what you carry you become.*

Matilda is already looking away. She has read this line before. She held it for three weeks the way someone holds a breath they are not sure they should release.

"That name has been waiting for a reader who could carry it without being consumed," she says. "You are that reader. The guard at the gate reads what they see. They do not read what they feel. Feel is not in their training. But the name in your hand — that name has weight in the Lattice. And the Lattice does not care about training."

The first guard looks up from her ledger. Her eyes find Quill across the market noise. Not a tracking look — an inventory look. The way someone catalogs what is present without knowing what it means. She makes a note. Something in the Lattice hum shifts, the way a frequency shifts when a new listener tunes in.

Quill folds the paper closed. The pen against the palm writes something before Quill decides to move — the nib pressing to the inside of the folded paper, adding a line that was not there a moment ago. Quill feels it happen but cannot stop it. The pen knows what it is doing and Quill is only the hand it moves through.

Matilda sees the pen move. She does not comment. She gathers her ledger and steps back from the stall, giving Quill a clear path to the gate.

"Walk slow," she says again, quieter now. "The Lattice is listening now. It will be listening harder when you pass through."

The gate hums. The guards wait. The pen is warm. The folded paper holds something that weighs nothing and means everything, and Quill is about to carry it straight into the place where names are processed and weighed and either released or held forever.

The child's finger is raised again, pointing at Quill from across the market. Still pointing. Still silent.

## Turn 5

Quill walks through the gate at the pace the market sets — not slow enough to signal fear, not fast enough to signal hurry. The pace of someone who belongs exactly where they are.

The guard's mark lands in the ledger. A single stroke, the kind that separates processing from passage, that says *seen* without saying *approved*. The name the pen wrote without being asked has been read by something that knows how to read what isn't written loud. The gate hum shifts — a frequency Quill feels in the joints more than the ears.

The guard who made the mark steps aside without comment. But the third guard — the one who stood back, hands folded, eyes on the ledger — crosses from the stall she's been watching and comes to Quill instead. She moves without urgency. She has the patience of someone who has never needed to hurry to catch what she was after.

"You walk slow," she says. Not a question. Not an accusation. The particular flatness of a voice trained to ask questions that don't sound like accusations. "The Lattice notices slow. The Lattice charges slow for what it carries." She looks at the notebook pressed against Quill's chest, then at the pen resting in Quill's open palm. "The pen writes what it wants. I have seen that before." She pauses. "Processing holds what the Lattice routes. You will be held for routing if you carry weight the routing cannot account for."

The pen presses warm. The name Verath sits where it settled — not Quill's to know yet, but Quill's to carry.

The guard waits. She is not asking. She is measuring the weight of a name against the weight of a question she has not yet decided whether to ask.
