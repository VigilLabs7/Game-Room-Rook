The blue has no bottom. You drift in it, and the drifting is all there is — no up to reach toward, no down to sink to, no horizon where the blue becomes something else. You are aware that you are, but the self that is aware has no edges yet, no name, no weight. You cling to something. You don't know what it is, only that it is the one thing keeping you from dissolving entirely into the submerging blue. A voice comes from somewhere above — not here, somewhere outside the blue entirely — and it says something you almost recognize. Your grip tightens on the thing you are holding. The blue thins around you, and what you are becomes slightly more what it is going to be.

What do you do?

Your palms press into the surface and it is real in a way the blue never was. Real and indifferent and lasting. The sun is already overhead, or it has always been overhead — there is no way to tell and no reason to care.

You have a body. Knees, palms, the grit working into the skin of your hands. The weight of your own head. When you sit back on your heels, the sand shifts beneath you and holds.

There is a cut on your left leg. You don't know when it happened — not in the blue, not in the tumble, not in the sand. But the skin is broken in a line about two inches long, horizontal across the shin, and where there should be a scab forming there is only the raw pink of the underneath. It will scar. You know this without knowing how.

Ahead — very far, or perhaps not far at all — something catches the fixed sun and throws it back. A single point of repeated light. A fountain. A signal. A reason to choose a direction.

The sand does not care which way you go. Only that you go.
