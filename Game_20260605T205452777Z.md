**Game Master:**

You run the sheet.

The press takes it the way it always takes it — smooth, even, the paper accepting the ink without hesitation. The Lore sheet comes out clean. Iron-black. One more name reclaimed from the empire's silence. You set it aside where it can dry and you pull the next form from the folder and your hands are moving before you are thinking, sorting the type, finding the letters, locking the form. The thread hums against your wrist. The work is the work and the work has a rhythm and the rhythm is yours.

You are three letters into the third name when the bell above the shop door rings.

Not the slow ring of a customer. Not the deliberate ring of someone who has decided to come in. A single, clipped sound — the kind of ring that means someone stopped in the doorway and looked around before committing to entering.

The shopkeeper's voice, from the front of the store, flat and unsurprised: "Good evening, officers."

You hear the click of boots on the floor. Three sets. The rhythm of men who are used to walking in formation and have stopped needing to think about it. You hear them spread out — one toward the counter, two toward the back of the store. Toward the door you locked.

You set another letter. The thread hums.

The press holds. The ink is where you left it. The folder is heavy and the room is yours and the lock is good.

One of the officers speaks. A man's voice, low, official: "We've had reports of unusual activity on this street. A press running after hours."

The shopkeeper's voice: "This is a general store. We close at dark. Whatever you heard, it wasn't this door."

A pause. The boots don't move. The officer speaks again: "You don't mind if we take a look?"

The shopkeeper: "I mind. But you don't work for me."

A sound at the back room door. Not the handle — the flat of a hand against the wood. Testing.

The lock holds.

You set another letter.
