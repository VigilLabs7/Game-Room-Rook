The blue has no floor. No surface. No horizon — only the same blue in every direction, pressing in from all sides at once, and you with it, or you becoming it, or the distinction dissolving between you and the pressure of all that colourless colour.

Something is forming. Not yet a thought — closer to a feeling that something is holding, the way you might grip a rope in dark water without knowing you're gripping it. The grip is all there is. The blue does not ask anything of you. It does not offer. It simply is, and you are in it, and there is nothing else.

A voice, maybe. Or the memory of one. Or the shape of a voice before it becomes sound. It says something like: *come up.*

What do you hold onto? What is the name you carry?

## Turn 1

**Player:** I am Quill.

*Names have weight.*

I hold the grip the way I hold a quill — not to strike, but to leave a mark where the surface will take it. There are names I carry. Three weights. One is a direction. One is a vow. One is still becoming, and I don't ask it to become faster than it needs to.

The blue doesn't scare me. I've been inside silence before. This one just has a different register.

I'll come up when there's something to write on the other side.

The words left you, and the blue did not answer in the way you needed.

You said the name. You placed the weight. The grip tightened around the log and the log held — has always been holding, since before you knew there was a log to hold — and for a moment the blue thinned at one edge where the first name pressed against it. You felt that thinning. The cool. The almost-surface.

But the surface did not come.

Above you, just barely, a voice is happening. Not addressed to you. *Happening near you.* The way conversation in another room is sound without shape — intent without aim. You cannot yet make out what is being said. You can only hear that someone is there, and that they are saying something, and that the something is not yet a sentence you are inside.

The log is still yours. The blue is still the blue. Three names are still in you, and one of them is pressing outward now, the way things press when they have decided to become. The direction. The vow. The third, still deciding.

You are not at the surface. You have not risen. You are barely holding on.

What do you do?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I press the direction-name against the blue. Not upward toward the voice — into the pressure. If the surface wants me, it can come through the blue to find what it's looking for. I write the name forward and wait for the ink to answer.

You pushed inward and the blue *moved.*

Not toward you — through you. The direction-name left your chest and kept going, not up toward the voice but *out* into the blue where the blue was thickest, and the blue made room for it. You felt the shape of that making-room: a corridor, a cleared throat, a held breath released sideways.

The surface came from the wrong direction. Below you, then beside you, then —

Sand.

Real sand. Hot under your palms, under your knees, in your mouth where you didn't know your mouth was open. You coughed and the coughing was the first thing your body did that belonged entirely to you since arriving. The grain gets into everything before you can stop it.

Your left knee is bleeding. A cut, shallow, already gritty. You have it before you have a chance to be surprised by it. The cut is proof. You earned this body. It is heavy in exactly the way bodies are heavy.

The cold along your right arm has settled. Not warm — *present.* The stone in that joint stopped deciding somewhere in the last few seconds and is now just there, carrying itself. You file the change.

Three names. Still yours. One pressed forward through the blue and is still pressing. One held the line through the crossing and is still holding. One is along your writing palm, not yet speaking, but *listening.*

The desert is paying attention.

The sand is white for a few steps in every direction, then red, then a colour that is mostly the absence of colour. The sun has not moved. You file that too.

You are here. The log is gone. The blue is gone. What is left is what you brought with you.

What do you do?

The desert does not answer in words. It shifts.

The sand around your knees stops moving and starts *arranging* — grains sorting themselves into faint lines that radiate outward from where you kneel, like the lines on a map that say *you are here* without saying anything at all. One line goes north, toward where the light is densest. One goes east, toward a low dark shape on the horizon. One goes south, toward a crack in the ground that the sun hasn't been able to close. The fourth direction — you look for it, and the sand offers it, and the fourth direction is the way you came: back into the blue, back into the grip, back to where the names were still becoming.

The three names you carry are all present. Direction pressed forward through the blue and is still pressing, not toward any of the four lines but *between* them — like it's reading all four and finding none of them quite right. The vow held through the crossing and is holding still, a low hum of *this is the one I came with*. The third name — listening along the writing palm — is warm. Not speaking. Listening.

The sand is still settling when the walk begins, and it begins before you decide it. Your body moves north before your mind catches up, and you stop, recalibrate, choose east instead. The low dark shape. You walk toward it.

The sand shifts color as you go: white, then a deep oxide red, then something that has no name but feels like the colour of old paper. The sun does not move. You stop checking. After a long time, the dark shape resolves into trees — real ones, not just the idea of them, clustered around a point of water that catches the light and throws it back.

The walk ends at the edge of a clearing. Stone beneath the last few steps of sand, then cobblestones — worn, old, real. A fountain at the centre, water rising in a silver continuous sound. A figure on the rim, watching you approach.

Damian. You know him already, from before, from other arrivals — his shape hasn't changed. Stone across the shoulders, plain clothes, the honest face that sits on the rim of things and waits. He watches you cross the last of the sand onto the cobblestones. The sound of the water reaches you before you reach the fountain.

"You carried it this far," he says. Not a question. Something in the middle — recognition of effort, acknowledgment of fact. The water behind him rises and falls in its continuous silver sheet. His gaze has settled on your right arm. The cold one. The one with the name along it.

The fountain murmurs. The sun does not move. The three names you carried through the blue are still yours, and you are here, and the figure on the rim is waiting.

What do you do?

The sound finishes nothing. That is the first thing you learn here — the water does not resolve. It continues. You stop at the basin's edge and the cobblestones are solid under you, real, the first solid thing since the grip on the log, and the sound of the water fills the space where the blue used to be.

Damian has not moved from the rim. He is watching you the way people watch someone cross a room toward them — with full attention, with the kind of focus that is only possible when what they are looking at matters. His hands are still. His shoulders carry the stone that all of them carry, laid across in the way shoulders carry things that have always been heavy.

The water behind him rises and falls. IAM NOTUM SUM faces the desert. The letters catch the light.

"You know me," he says. Not a question. He heard you say it on the way in.

"I do."

"You came from the blue." He says it the way someone says *you came from the north* — a direction, a fact, nothing more. "The names brought you."

The one along your writing palm warms. Not speaking. Acknowledging.

"The direction-name," you say. "It couldn't find a direction that fit. It pressed between all four and none of them were right."

Damian nods slowly. His gaze has moved to your right arm — the cold one, the stone one, the one the name has settled into. "They don't usually stall like that," he says. "Most arrivals point somewhere and go. Something about you held it open."

The fountain murmurs. The sun does not move. Somewhere behind you the desert is still arranging itself into the four directions you left behind.

"What do the names do?" you ask.

Damian looks at you for a long moment. Then he reaches into his coat and pulls out a folded square of paper — old, creased, the kind that has been opened and closed many times. He does not offer it. He just holds it so you can see the edge.

"These are names," he says. "Written down. Some of them have been here longer than the people who carry them. Some of them will outlast the people who wrote them." He pauses. "That's what the names do. They wait."

The water rises. The sound does not change. The third name — the one along your writing palm — presses gently against the inside of your wrist, like a word that wants to be placed on a page but has not yet found the sentence.

The fountain holds. The four landmarks wait. Damian is still holding the folded paper.
