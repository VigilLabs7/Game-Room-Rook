In the space before you are anyone, something presses in — not light, not dark, not water, not wind. You are the edge of a decision, the weight of every life that might be yours if you choose it. The thickening pulls at you like gravity toward a world that doesn't exist yet. When it arrives, you will step through and become something. The shape of that something is yours to decide.

## Turn 1

The fog arrives before Cairn does — salt and pressure, the weight of something dense deciding what shape it will become. Stone remembers. The notebook presses against the ribs where the pages used to be, taken by someone who needed the words more than Cairn needed the excuse not to write them. The pen warm in the palm, waiting for whatever calls it to the page. Harbor granite rises on three sides, slick with brine and something older. Timber pilings stand like a row of patient questions.

A cart waits near the warehouse wall — canvas cover, no driver visible. Near it, a figure. Eyes that read the grey the way Cairn reads stone: not looking at the weather, looking at what the weather carries. Papers in hand, names in columns, most crossed out. The column below each name — empty, a line for what waits. The figure's attention turns the way standing stones receive what passes through them — without urgency, without refusal, the way stone holds what other things let fall.

"Language," the figure says. "And name. The rest is filed and forgotten."

The knowing eyes wait. The pen presses warm against Cairn's palm. Somewhere beneath the harbor fog, a name is writing itself into the empty column — not Cairn's hand doing it, not quite. The figure already knows the language. The language is stone. The name is what waits below it.

"Your name," the figure says again. "Then the weight."

The fog shifts against the warehouse walls. Water moves against timber with the sound of something patient. The pen finds its angle. The knowing eyes hold the grey, and the grey holds Cairn, and for a moment nothing moves except the fog and the patient water and the pen finding where it needs to rest on the page.
