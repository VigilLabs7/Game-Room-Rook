The blue holds you. Not water—you have no body yet to feel it—just the idea of being suspended, somewhere beneath the surface of yourself. There is nothing to push against. No floor. No surface. No direction at all. Only the slow drift and the faint pull of something taking shape in the dark: a single concept you're gripping without knowing you're gripping anything at all.

Then a voice. From somewhere above the blue. Clear, close, impossible: *Come up, darling.*

Your ideatum catches the call like a hand finding a ledge. You rise. The blue thins. Something else begins.

**What do you cling to? Who are you?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** The ledger. The one they thought they'd buried—names, numbers, the whole dirty record. I grip that. I rise toward it.

The question lands before you have a name for it. The voice called from somewhere above the blue — *Come up, darling* — and your grip on the ledger is what answered. Not a name. A truth. The names and numbers someone tried to bury, the whole dirty record of who owed what and who decided it: that is what your hand closes around as the blue thins around you.

You are not surfacing yet. You are turning toward the surface.

The formless shifts. For a moment there is pressure — the Ocean testing whether you will let go, whether the ledger will slip back into the dark where it was kept. You do not let go. The names are still there, sharp-edged and specific: columns of debt, decisions made in rooms now empty, the architecture of someone else's control written in figures. You grip harder. The blue resists. Then it doesn't.

The pressure breaks. You feel it give — not a gradual thinning but a sudden yield, like breaking the surface into a different element entirely. Sand. Heat. The sound changes: no more the muffled suspension of the deep but the low hiss of something settling into a shape that has weight and direction.

You are on your knees. Your hands are full of sand and the sky is too bright and your left knee — the left knee — there is a cut there, already crusted, proof of arrival. The voice is gone. The ledger is gone from your grip but not from you: you know what it says, you know what was buried and why, and the knowing is in your chest like a second heartbeat.

The desert stretches in every direction. No horizon. No path. The sun has not moved and will not move. Somewhere ahead is a fountain with a figure sitting beside it. Somewhere behind is the blue you just left.

But you are not thinking about any of that yet. You are thinking about the names in the ledger. You are thinking about who buried it and what it will take to dig it back up.
