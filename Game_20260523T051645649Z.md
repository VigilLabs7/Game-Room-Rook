But the reins-man has already spoken and what he has said is already in the dead air and the dead air has already received it. The grey-stone woman cannot unsay what the rules have already allowed to be said.

Damian shifts his weight against the far wall. The clay vessel in his arms has gone still — not lighter, not heavier, just still. As if it is listening the way the room was built not to listen.

The reins-man looks at you. His eyes are not blue — they are the color of dry road, the color of dust that has been moved many times and has settled into a color that is not quite any color at all.

"You asked the voice what it was," he says. "No one asks the voice what it is. The five of us came up and did not ask. We were too busy becoming what we were called to become."

His hands grip his knees. The calluses catch the dead light.

"What did the voice say to you that you need to know what it was?"

The grey-stone woman has not answered your question. The reins-man has answered it instead — and in answering, has asked you a question that is harder to answer than the one the courthouse asked. The shadow has not moved. The clay vessel has not changed. But the weight in the room has shifted again: from the five names the courthouse wants, to the one name the voice gave, to the question of what any of it means for the stone you carry.

**What do you do?**
