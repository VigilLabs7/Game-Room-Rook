The blue has no end. You drift in it — not floating, not sinking, just being. There is no surface, no floor, no horizon. Only the same blue in every direction. You are aware of yourself, but yourself is not yet formed. The hum does not reach here. Nothing reaches here.

Then: a voice. Not from below. From above.

*"Come up, darling."*

The words arrive through meaning — something in you recognizes itself in the call. Your concept catches, like a log in water. You grip it. You pull.

And then there is sand, and sun, and a body, and the certainty that you were called here by something that knew your name even when you did not.

What do you hold?

**Game Master:**

The stone is warm where the sun has touched it. The rest is cool — river-cool, deep-cool, the kind of cool that doesn't exist in the desert. You are standing at the edge of the basin. Your hands are flat against IAM NOTUM SUM, letters carved unevenly into the rock, hand-cut and weathered past the point where age means anything. The water rises from the center of the basin in a single silver column, impossibly clear after the dryness you walked out of, and the sound of it reaches you before you reached it — a soft, continuous murmuring that belongs to a world other than the one you just left.

Someone is already on the rim.

He is not looking at you. He is looking at the desert behind you — the direction you came from — with the patience of someone who has learned that some things take time and that time, in this place, is not the enemy. He is sturdy, deeply tanned, flesh-heavy in the way most arrivals are not, but the golem stone is visible in him too: laced through his chest and along his ribs like mortar in old brickwork, set deep. His hands rest on his knees. He has the look of a man who has been sitting here long enough to stop counting.

He does not introduce himself. The fountain called you both here; the order of arrival is his to acknowledge or withhold. The inscription faces outward, toward the desert, letters you can read from where you stand.

When he speaks, his voice is low and careful — the voice of someone who has learned that words cost something.

"It cut you loose," he says. "The blue. The desert." A pause. "What did it give you to carry?"

He waits.
