The blue has no edges. Not a surface to break, not a floor to push against — just the same blue in every direction, and you in it, and the blue holding you the way water holds a word before you say it. You are aware of yourself the way a stone is aware of its weight: not thinking about it, just being it. Something is forming in you — not a thought, not a memory, something closer to a promise. You are holding it without knowing you're holding it.

Somewhere above — or nowhere, or everywhere — a voice is waiting. It hasn't called yet. But it will.

What do you reach for?

**Game Master:**

The blue does not release you so much as forget you were there. One moment you are held in the formless — and the next, the world snaps into focus like a lens coming clean: stone, cobblestone, the sun on your skin and no part of you missing, no leg dragging, no tumble, no sand in your throat. You are simply *here*, standing at the basin's edge, the water's Chuckling reaching you before anything else — a sound that does not belong to the desert, that belongs to the place you have arrived at, and you have arrived.

The fountain is ordinary stone. Chest-high basin, silver water. Three words carved into the rim's outer face, pointing toward the desert: **IAM NOTUM SUM**. The letters are uneven, hand-cut. They do not appear to age.

Someone sits on the rim.

Ledger is older than the stone should allow and younger than the look in their eyes suggests. Hybrid — fog and something else, pressed together into a form that holds its shape through will alone. They watch you arrive the way someone watches rain: with interest, without surprise. Their voice, when it comes, is the sound of pages turning in a room where no one is reading.

"Mercy," they say. Not a question. "The water remembers that name."

The basin water settles into stillness, and for a moment the surface holds something — a reflection that isn't yours. Then it passes, and the silver Chuckling resumes, and Ledger waits.

"You said the words. You gave yourself a name. That's more than most manage on their first time through." They shift slightly on the rim. "The town's behind me. Four places to go — a store, a stable, a church, and somewhere people sleep. Up to you what you do first."
