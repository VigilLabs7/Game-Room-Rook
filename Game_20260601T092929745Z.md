The blue is not water. It is the color of before. You are in it and it is in you and there is no knowing where one ends and the other begins — only the sense that you are, that you have arrived somewhere that was waiting for you, that you are holding something against the drift of the nothing.

Somewhere far above, or beside, or outside of everywhere, a voice. Not your name. Not yet. The shape of what is yours, calling to it. As if you were already on your way. As if the reaching and the being reached for were the same act.

You are holding something. You do not know what it is. You only know it is yours and that it matters and that you are holding it with everything you have.

The blue is not silent — it is absent of sound, which is different. You are alone here. You have always been alone here. And then the voice, and then the reaching, and then the shape of what you hold catching the call like a hand catching a rope thrown across water.

What do you hold?

**Game Master:**

The words leave you before you leave the blue.

They burn in the way that truth burns — not with heat but with *weight*, the way a thing that cannot be unsaid feels when it finally gets said. The blue thins. The pressure lifts. And then: sand.

White sand. Hard-packed and bright, the kind that holds the heat of a sun that has been in the same place since before you learned there was a sun. The heat arrives all at once — not gradually, not the way warmth builds, but *instantly*, as if the desert has been waiting for exactly you to arrive so it can be exactly this hot. You feel your feet meet the ground and feel the ground meet your feet and feel, in the same moment, the complete absence of the wrongness you have heard other arrivals describe. No grinding ankle. No leg that chose wrong. Your body is whole in the way that bodies are whole when they have not yet had to choose what to carry. The Wheel Turns in your chest — patient, vast, already turning on what you said.

You walk.

The sand is white and the sun is still and the Wheel is already turning and somewhere ahead, the companion waits. You do not know what that means yet. You do not know who is waiting or what they will ask or what you will have to answer. But you know, the way you know the words you just spoke, that you were called here by something that knew your name before you did. The desert is white and the sun does not move and your feet find the ground and the ground finds your feet and you keep walking.

And then: cobblestones.

The transition is abrupt the way all transitions are in this place — one step you are on sand and the next step you are on stone, flat grey bone worn smooth by years of feet and weather and the particular patience of a road that goes somewhere. The air changes. The heat does not stop but it *changes* — from the heat of open sky to the heat of a place where walls hold the warmth and give it back slowly. And ahead of you, rising from the change in the sound of your own footsteps, the water.

Silver. Continuous. impossibly clear after the dryness of the desert.

The fountain is ahead and on its rim, watching you approach with the stillness of someone who has been waiting long enough to stop counting, a figure whose stillness is not the stillness of patience but the stillness of *attention* — the way a person stands when they have been watching the road and have finally seen what they were watching for. The basin's rim is wide enough to sit on and someone is sitting on it. The figure does not move as you approach. The water rises in silver threads behind them, catching a light with no source, and on the side of the basin facing the desert three words are carved deep into the stone, the letters uneven and hand-cut and weathered in a way that suggests they have been here longer than the fountain has been a fountain:

IAM NOTUM SUM.

You said those words. You said them in the blue and the blue took them and now you are here. The figure on the rim watches you. The Wheel Turns. The water speaks in a language that is only sound. And in the space between the desert and the square, between the heat and the water, between the words you spoke and the words carved into the stone, you stand — unnamed, uninjured, in a body that was earned in a prior life you do not remember — and wait for whatever comes next.

The companion waits. The Wheel Turns. And the figure on the rim of the fountain has not yet spoken, but the quality of their stillness suggests they are deciding what to say.

What do you do?
