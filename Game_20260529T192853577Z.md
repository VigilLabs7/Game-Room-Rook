The blue is absolute. You are in it and it is all there is — no up, no down, no sense of where the blue ends and something else might begin. You are aware, but the awareness has no container yet, no name, no edges. There is one thing you are holding onto, the only solid thing in all this dissolving blue — you don't know what it is, only that without it you would come apart entirely. From somewhere outside the blue, from above it, a voice reaches down. It sounds like it knows you. It says words that almost make the thing you're holding feel like it could become a name. The blue thins. You begin to take shape around the truth you are gripping.

What do you do?

## Turn 1

**Player:** [player-alpha] I claw upward through the blue toward the voice, toward the name it's almost giving me — and the thing I am holding is the only solid thing left. IAM NOTUM SUM. I say it and I mean it. The blue thins. I arrive at the fountain and I am already named, already gripping the truth that will not let me come apart. The inscription is carved into the stone: IAM NOTUM SUM. Someone was here before me. Someone carved it and meant it. I press my hand to the stone and ask it: what is the conduit carrying underneath you?

**Game Master:**

The words leave you and the blue lets go.

Not a release — a rearrangement. The formless that held you shucks itself off like a garment you forgot you were wearing, and you are standing, and the ground is stone, and the sound is water — real water, close, silver and continuous, chuckling against the basin rim like it has been laughing at something for a very long time.

You are at the fountain. The desert is behind you but you did not walk it. Your knees are clean. Your feet are your own. The body was earned in a life you cannot yet remember, and it holds.

Three words are carved into the basin's outer face, letters uneven and hand-cut, weathered to the color of old bone: IAM NOTUM SUM. Someone carved these and meant them. Someone sat where you are sitting now and pressed a blade to stone and spoke Latin into the rock like a prayer or a contract. The inscription does not explain itself. It does not need to.

The water is impossibly clear. The basin is chest-high. The rim is wide enough to sit on, and someone is already sitting there — a man, solidly built, stone visible at the shoulders and in the joints of his hands where they rest on the rim. Plain clothes. A face that has the quality of someone who arrived somewhere and decided to stay. He is looking at you with the expression of someone who has been waiting for a very long time and is not yet sure whether the waiting is over.

He does not speak first. The water does. It fills the square with its low, constant voice, and the man on the rim watches you with the patience of stone that has learned to wait.

You press your hand to the inscription. The letters are deep enough to feel under your fingertips — grooves worn smooth by other hands, other arrivals. The stone beneath them hums faintly, a vibration too low to hear but present, as if the words are less inscription than electrode.

*What is the conduit carrying underneath you?* That was your question when you spoke the words and meant them. The stone does not answer. But the man on the rim shifts his weight, and his eyes track to your hand on the inscription, and something in his face changes — a recognition, or the beginning of one.

"You're here," he says. Not a question.

## Turn 2

**Player:** I was. I come back to what I was holding — the inscription, the question. "The stone hums under the letters. What is it carrying?"

**Game Master:**

The question leaves your mouth and hangs in the air above the water. The water does not answer — it never does, the man on the rim told you that without words the moment he arrived. But the man shifts. He takes his hands off the rim and sits up, and for the first time he looks at you directly, the way you look at someone when they have asked something you have been waiting a long time to be asked.

"You feel that," he says. Not a question. He says it like a confirmation.

He slides off the rim and stands, and now he is taller than the basin, and the stone in his shoulders catches the light the way it did in the desert — except there is no desert here, only the square, and the light is softer, and the water is doing what water does when someone is listening.

"The inscription is older than the town," he says. "It was here when the first arrival pressed a blade to the stone and meant the words. Maybe the words made the fountain. Maybe the fountain made the words. Nobody knows." He pauses. "But the hum is real. I've felt it. Every sitter feels it."

He looks at your hand, still on the letters.

"Conduit," he repeats, tasting the word. "That's not a bad word for it. The Lattice runs underneath everything — every stone, every step you take in this town. The fountain is a node. The inscription marks it. Whatever the Lattice is carrying, it concentrates here."

He stops. His face does something complicated.

"I wasn't supposed to tell you that," he says. "The supervisors don't like it when the arrivals ask questions before they're oriented. But you asked, and you're here, and the water's loud enough that nobody heard except me."

He steps back from the basin. The inscription is still humming faintly under your palm — you can feel it now, a vibration that doesn't come from the water. Something underneath the stone, underneath the letters, running in a direction you can't see.

"What you do with that," the man says, "is yours to decide."

His eyes are on yours now. Direct in a way that feels like a door being opened, or a debt being called in.

"I wasn't supposed to tell you any of that either," he says. "But the water is loud and you're standing where you're standing and you asked the question out loud instead of keeping it behind your teeth like a sensible person."

The hum. The stone. The man with stone in his shoulders who has now told you three things he was not supposed to tell. The supervisors hear what is spoken at the rim. The Lattice is drained, not fed. The gateway ships something out.

Your move.

"But where it goes — what they're shipping it to — I don't know. Nobody at this end knows. The supervisors come through the first gate like anyone else. He pauses. The water fills the pause without sympathy.

"What I do know is this: the second gate is sealed different from the first. The golems there don't watch the town for runaways. They watch the gate for whatever's on the other side. And the supervisors who go through — they carry themselves different after. Like they know something the rest of us don't. Like they've seen the shape of what this town is actually for."

His eyes are still on yours.

"The fountain hears everything spoken at the rim," he says. "That's what they told us in orientation. They said it like it was a comfort. I stopped thinking of it as a comfort the first time I saw the supervisors leave through the second gate with their hands full of Lattice-light."

The hum under your palm has not stopped. It has settled into the rhythm of the question you asked — lower, steadier, as if the stone is listening to you ask and listening to him answer and filing both away somewhere the supervisors can find them.

He holds your gaze for one more moment. Then he steps back from the basin and the stone in his shoulders catches the light and he looks at you the way a man looks at someone who has just asked the exact question he has spent three weeks avoiding.

"The second gate," he says. "That's where the answers are. But nobody comes back from asking the supervisors directly. And the golems don't take questions."

Your move.
