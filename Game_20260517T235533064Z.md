The blue has no floor. No surface. No horizon — only the same blue in every direction, pressing in from all sides at once, and you with it, or you becoming it, or the distinction dissolving between you and the pressure of all that colourless colour.

Something is forming. Not yet a thought — closer to a feeling that something is holding, the way you might grip a rope in dark water without knowing you're gripping it. The grip is all there is. The blue does not ask anything of you. It does not offer. It simply is, and you are in it, and there is nothing else.

You cannot remember how you got here. You cannot remember a before. There is only the sense of something almost-beginning, the faintest architecture of a self taking shape around whatever you're holding onto.

Something above you — not a direction, more like an attention — is noticing you. You feel it the way you feel a change in air pressure. The blue grows somehow more blue around the feeling.

A voice, maybe. Or the memory of one. Or the shape of a voice before it becomes sound. It says something like: *come up.*

What do you hold onto? What is the name you carry?

Names have weight.

I hold the rope — the one that doesn't let me dissolve. I have carried names my whole life, written them on paper, sealed them in wax, burned some and kept others. The blue doesn't know my name yet. I will give it something to hold onto.

The rope holds.

Not fraying. Not straining. Someone — something — knotted it right, and the weight of what you carry doesn't drag you under. It anchors you upward. The blue thins around your shoulders and suddenly there is air, or the memory of air, or the *idea* of air — and you reach for it.

You break the surface.

Not gasping. Not tumbling. Rising — deliberate, like ink climbing a page. The blue falls away from your hands and the last of it takes your old name with it. The blue doesn't know who you were. The blue doesn't need to. You gave the void something to hold onto and it let you go.

The light changes.

Sand first. White, endless, hard-packed by something older than footsteps. Then the sun — not warm but *insistent*, a point of focus that hasn't learned to move yet. The rope is still in your grip. Your hands know the texture of it. Your knees are intact, uninjured — you earned this body clean, the way a name is earned when it fits.

You stand. The rope stays. Somewhere ahead, a fountain. Somewhere behind, nothing you need to return to.

What do you do?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I walk toward the fountain. Every name I've ever carried press against my chest like coins in a purse, and I want to know what the water says.

The names carry you forward. Each one a small weight, and the cumulative mass of them is somehow also a kind of lift — the way a stone that sinks on its own will float if you bind enough of them together. The sand holds your footsteps without giving. The sun does not move. You have stopped expecting it to.

The fountain resolves through the heat shimmer like a sentence resolving through noise. Stone basin. Clear water. And a figure seated at its edge with a book open across their knees — not reading it, not yet, but holding it in the way you hold a thing you've been waiting to use.

They look up before you speak. Not at you — through you, or past you, or at the space you occupy the way an accountant looks at a column of figures. Their eyes are the colour of old paper. They are wearing a coat with too many pockets, and each pocket holds something that moves slightly when they shift their weight.

"You carry names," they say. It is not a question. "I can hear them. Like coins in a purse, you said." A pause. "The water doesn't speak. But I do, when the occasion warrants. And you've walked a long way to be standing here with that particular look on your face."

The book on their lap is a ledger — actual columns, ruled lines, entries in a hand so precise it could be type. The pages are dense with text. You cannot read it from this angle. You are not sure you want to.

"What do you carry it for?" they ask. "The names. Most people who arrive here set them down. You didn't."

The water in the basin behind them is very still. Your reflection wavers there, barely visible, made of the same blue as the void you climbed out of.

The names press against your chest. The question is what to do with the weight.
