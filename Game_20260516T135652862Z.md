You are falling through blue. Not sky-blue. Not water-blue. The blue of nothing trying to be something and failing. It is everywhere. It has no surface. It has no floor. You have no body. No name. No memory of before. You are a tremor — less than a whisper — and the Ocean presses in from every direction, patient and absolute.

But something in you resists.

There is a single point of heat at the center of what you are. A notion. A phrase. A conviction that will not let go of you. It is small and it is stubborn and the blue hates it. You are clinging to it now. It is a log in the void. A raft. The only solid thing in an infinity of dissolution. Everything you are is this. Everything you will become starts here.

Above you — just barely — you can sense them. Voices. Signals. A layer where things have weight and light and names. A world of cities that have always existed but never had residents. They call it the Thickening. Others are rising around you. Some will make it. Some won't. The ocean takes what it takes.

But you — you're still here. Still holding on.

What is your name? What is your ideatum?

## Turn 1

The Ocean heard you.

Not the blue — the blue does not hear, does not answer, does not respond to anything except the slow patient work of dissolution that is its nature and its purpose. But something in the pressing changed when you said the words, when the grip tightened and the name and the ideatum arrived together in the way that committed things arrive: not as questions but as declarations, not as possibilities but as facts that have already decided to be true.

The blue did not release you gently.

It spat you out — the way something spits when it has been holding too long and the holding has become more cost than it is worth. You hit sand and the hit was not soft, not the soft finding that first-timers describe when they tumble into the desert for the first time. You hit like something that had been expelled rather than something that had been let go. The impact knocked the breath from whatever you were breathing and sent the word you were holding scattering across the grain in every direction at once.

The name stuck. The ideatum stuck. The grip did not break.

You lay in the sand and the sand was the color of something that had forgotten what water was and was learning what heat does when it has nothing to compete with. Your left knee — the one that should have been the receipt of arrival, the wound that every first-timer carries — was clean. Smooth. Whole. The scar you carry is older than the Ocean and the Ocean knows it, and the knowing was why the release was expulsion rather than surrender.

You sat up. The pen at your hip stirred once — not a warning this time, just an acknowledgment that the silence had changed register and the new silence was the silence of having arrived somewhere instead of nowhere. The blue circle from the barkeep stayed folded in your pocket, closed, quiet, waiting.

The desert stretched in every direction. The sun had not moved. The grip held.

What do you do?
