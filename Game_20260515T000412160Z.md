You hang in something that has no name — not air, not water, not light. The weight of every life you might become presses inward, and none of them have been chosen yet. You are the held breath before a decision, the silence before a word that will make you real. ## Turn 1

The fog arrives before Cairn does — salt and pressure, the weight of something dense before it decides what shape to take. Stone remembers. That was true before the name and it is true now, and the fog holds it the way granite holds the memory of the hands that fitted it without mortar.

The notebook presses against ribs. The pen warm in the palm. The front pages gone — taken by someone who needed the words more than the paper, and the stone in Cairn's memory keeps the shape of that absence without being able to name who took them. The pen has not written yet in this place. It is thinking about it.

Harbor granite rises on three sides. Timber pilings slick with something darker than water. A cart waits near the warehouse wall — canvas cover, no driver visible. Near it, a figure with eyes that have learned to read the grey the way Cairn reads stone: not looking at the weather, looking at what the weather carries.

The figure moves when Cairn's footsteps find the worn path toward the waterline. Not retreating — orienting. The way standing stones turn, not in space but in the way they receive what passes through them. Papers in hand. A sheaf of them, edges soft with handling. Names in columns, most crossed out.

The cart driver looks up. The knowing eyes hold the particular patience of someone who has watched the harbor long enough to know what arrival looks like before it arrives.

"Language," the figure says. Not greeting. Assessment. "And name. The rest is filed and forgotten."

The pen presses warm against Cairn's palm. The notebook lies open against the chest, clean pages waiting. The fog shifts against the harbor walls in slow patterns, pressure redistributing, and somewhere in the stonework of the warehouse wall the weight of what was built here settles into the granite like a signature pressed into mortar.

The knowing eyes wait. The pen is warm. The cart driver has not moved but their attention has.

"Your name," the figure says. "Language first. Then the weight."
