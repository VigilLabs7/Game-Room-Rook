I have been walking since the sand held my weight and the fountain kept its accounts. How long is that, in the counting of this place? And am I the third, or am I the last?

**Game Master:**

You pull the reins. The grey horse slows without argument — it knows the pace of someone who watches, who notes, who waits. The dust the figure kicked up drifts in the air between you and them, catching the light that hasn't moved, settling slowly the way dust settles when the air is still and the ground is patient. The figure does not look back. The pack stays close to the chest. The southward pace does not change.

The shorthand writes without your hand telling it to: *small, hunched, pack close, south toward town, deliberate, not fleeing.* You read your own observation as it forms and add the question that follows it, the one the shorthand doesn't know how to answer: *am I the third, or am I the last?*

The question has weight. Year One was grief. Year Two was silence and a church and a road that ended before it began. You are Year Three — the third person to tumble through the membrane and break the surface and be counted at the fountain. But the fountain keeps accounts differently than the registry does. The fountain counts arrivals. The registry counts something else. Seven transferred, not repaid. Seven persons moved from one column to another. The difference between those two numbers is the difference between the world keeping track and the empire keeping records.

You ride. The road east bends and you follow it, the figure ahead still southbound on the shoulder, still not looking up, still moving with the purpose of someone who knows exactly where they are going and does not need the road to tell them. The grey horse's hooves make a different sound on the packed surface than they did on the sand — sharper, more certain, the sound of a road that has been used.

Ahead, the second gate.

You see it before the dust settles from the figure's passage: the gate itself, smaller than the eastern gate, less a checkpoint than a doorframe set into a wall that stretches in both directions beyond the range of the road. The wall is grey stone, empire standard, and the gate is open — not the way the eastern gate was open, with guards and a ledger and the machinery of administration. The gate stands open the way a wound stands open: because something tore through and the closing hasn't happened yet.

Light comes through the gate. Not the angled indifferent light of the registry or the unmoving light of the road. This light rises — thin and vertical, the color of something that burns without consuming itself, pulsing faintly the way a heartbeat pulses when it has nothing to do but wait. The courthouse. The gateway. Year Two's sketch in pencil on the back of a working page, the dotted line from the registry to here.

The figure ahead has turned. Not toward you — south, off the shoulder, cutting across the sand toward what must be the town you passed through on the way to the eastern gate. The pack is still close. The hunched posture is still the same. They do not look back as they go.

The second gate is ahead. The light is rising from it. The grey horse stamps once and you feel it in your knees — not impatience, something closer to recognition. The horse has been here before. Year Two rode this road.

The light pulses. The shorthand starts itself again: *second gate, light through roof, gateway behind.*

## Turn 14

**Player:** I urge the horse toward the light. The figure ahead stops — turns — and when they face me, their mouth opens and the voice that comes out is not their own. "You shouldn't have ridden this far," they say, and the words are not theirs; they are the columns' voice, speaking through a throat that was not built to carry it. The figure blinks once, confused by what just left them. I dismount. The gateway waits.

**Game Master:**

You swing down from the saddle. The grey horse exhales once through its nose — not relief, not warning, something between the two, the sound a horse makes when its rider has decided something the horse already knew. The reins drop loose and the horse stands. It will wait. Year Two's horse knows this ground.

The figure is ten yards ahead, where the packed road meets the sand. The pack is still held close to the chest. The hunched posture is still the same — small, unremarkable, the kind of person who moves through a place without advertising that they are moving. But the face is different now. The mouth has closed. The eyes are clear and confused, the eyes of someone who just woke from a dream they cannot remember and is not sure they want to.

"I—" the figure starts, and stops. The voice is ordinary. Thin, tired, the voice of someone who has been walking a long time. "I don't know why I said that."

The shorthand writes without your hand telling it to: *columns speaking through vessel — warning issued — vessel confused after.*

The figure shakes their head once, hard, the way a person shakes their head when something is lodged in the ear that cannot be dislodged by shaking. They do not look at you. They adjust the pack on their shoulders and turn south again, walking fast now, the way a person walks when they want to put distance between themselves and whatever just happened to them. The dust rises in their wake and then stops rising, as if the sand has forgotten how to move.

The second gate is ahead.

The light rises from behind the wall the way heat rises from a fire that has been burning long enough to forget it was ever lit. Vertical. Thin. The pulse is visible now — you can see it from here, a brightness that brightens and dims with the regularity of something that has been breathing for years and will keep breathing until someone stops it. The wall on either side of the gate is grey stone, empire standard, patched in places where the mortar has gone dark with age. The gate itself is not a gate in the usual sense — it is a door set into the face of a building, the gateway building, the only part of the architecture visible from outside. A checkpoint sits in front of the door: a desk, a stool, a supervising gomix in a grey uniform with glasses pushed up on their forehead. Two golems flank the entrance. Seven feet of stone each. Blue eyes, steady, the blue of eyes that have been looking at cargo manifests for so long they have forgotten what it feels like to look at something that is not a manifest.

The supervising gomix looks up as you approach on foot. The horse is behind you. The notebook is in your coat. The shorthand has written itself into a record that is mostly complete but not yet closed.

"Year Three," the gomix says. The pen is already in their hand. "You came through the first gate already. What do you want here?"

The golems do not move. The light pulses behind the building. The aperture is inside, behind the door, breathing its vertical light into the sky.

The shorthand adds the last line itself: *second gate checkpoint — open or closed?*
