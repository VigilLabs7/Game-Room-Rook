You have the idea of a body, the memory of having had one, and you cling to it because there is nothing else. Something is taking shape in the drift — not a thought, not a word, but a shape that fits in the mouth, that your throat wants to form. You don't know what it is yet. You will know when it catches.

What do you hold onto?

**Game Master:**

You reach, and the reaching changes things.

The shape tightens as your fingers close on it. Not solid — not yet — but dense with the particular pressure of something that has been waiting to be held. Your grip makes it real in a way the blue's drift has not yet managed. The nothing has weight. You feel that immediately. What you are holding is not empty.

The blue thins where your hand is. Around your wrist the current is older than the drift — pulling back the way something pulls when it has been pushed past its edge and is finding its way home. You hold harder. The thinned place thins further. You feel the shape of what you are holding sharpen, become more itself, and the blue registers the change by going quieter around you.

Not silence. The blue is never silent. But a different quality of sound — the way a room sounds different when someone is listening in it.

Above you, just barely, a voice is happening. Not addressed to you. *Happening near you.* The way conversation in another room is happening — sound without shape, intent without aim. You can hear it because you are holding still, and the stillness makes you available to things you would not hear if you were gripping.

You are not at the surface yet. You have your hand on the thing that will get you there. But you have not yet decided whether to hold it, or let it go, or what you are willing to become in the holding.

The log is still yours. The drift is still pulling. The voice is still calling.

What do you do?

**Game Master:**

## Turn 3

**Game Master:**

The sand takes you.

That is the first thing. Not resistance — acceptance. The red dust rises around your boots and settles behind you and the desert receives every step like a ledger recording a debit. The columns must balance. You feel it in your ankles now, in the ache of the body that arrived whole and is learning what whole means in this place. The sun does not move. The shadow of the ridge does not grow. You walk toward a fixed point in a fixed sky and the walking is its own answer.

The ridge sharpens as you close on it. The stone is older than the dust — grey rock veined with something darker, dark enough to be shadow but too steady to be anything the sun is doing. The old border. You knew that without knowing how you knew it, the way you know the shape of a word you have not yet spoken. The empire redrew it. Before the redraw this ridge was a threshold, a place where one jurisdiction ended and another began. Now it is just stone in sand, and the sand on both sides is the same sand, and the sky above is the sky the empire could not redraw.

You crest the ridge.

Below you — in the valley the ridge was hiding — a town. Four landmarks visible from here: the general store to the north, the stable to the east, the church to the west, and in the center, the fountain. Water rising. Stone figures around it. The town is smaller than you expected. The fountain is larger. Silver water climbing and falling and climbing again in the same column, the same rhythm, the rhythm of something that has been going since before you arrived and will go after you are gone.

And on the rim of the fountain, a figure.

You cannot see their face from here. You can see that they are looking in your direction. They have been watching the ridge since before you crested it. They knew you would come before you knew you were coming, and the knowing sits in the way they hold their body — still, patient, the stillness of someone who has decided to wait rather than to seek.

What do you do?

**Game Master:**

The figure turns.

Not quickly — the turn is deliberate, unhurried, the way someone turns when they have been expecting the moment and have no reason to rush it. The water still rises behind them. The inscription faces the desert. The letters catch the light the way things catch light when they have been waiting longer than memory.

He is solid in the way that people who carry duty are solid — not tall, not short, but present in a way that makes the air feel more occupied than it did before. Stone through the shoulders and spine where flesh would slack, visible in the forearms and throat, the golem-work that this world puts into the bodies that arrive here. His eyes are the color of something that has been tested and holds. He looks at you the way someone looks at a ledger they are checking — carefully, completely, looking for the thing that does not balance.

"Nobody sent me," he says. His voice is the kind of voice that carries weight without raising volume. "The fountain summons. I answered. That is how it works."

He gestures at the rim beside him. Not an invitation — a statement of availability. The stone is wide enough for both of you if you chose to sit.

"The columns must balance," he says. Not a question. A recognition. He heard it when you crossed the ridge, or he felt it in the way the sand accepted your steps, or he has been sitting here long enough to know what the balancing sounds like when it approaches. "You carry that truth. I can see it."

He does not ask what it means to you. The what-it-means is yours. He only recognizes that it is there.

"Names are not required here," he says. "But they help. The town will ask your name. I am asking because I would rather know it than not."

The water still rises. The sun still does not move. The inscription reads IAM NOTUM SUM and has since before either of you arrived.

"Who are you," he says, "before the town gives you a second one?"
