You are nowhere, and the nowhere has texture — a pressure that builds without wind, a weight that has no floor to press against. Forms drift past like memories of lives, each one close enough to feel before it fades. The thickening is about to catch you. You can sense it the way you sense something about to be said. When you arrive, the world will begin.

## Turn 1

The harbor water moves like it remembers something heavier than tide. Timber pilings dark with age, salt and old fish, the particular desperation of goods that need selling before the season turns. A man with blue-gray eyes passes you on the dock and does not look at you. You feel the pressure of all the names this place has swallowed, and you wait.

## Turn 2

The fog gives way to you the way stone gives way to what presses against it — not defeated, just moved aside. A figure waits near the cart, patient as something geological, and when they turn their eyes hold a weight that reads like an old knowing. They speak a phrase with an accent you cannot place: arrivals here are sorted at the first gate, language and a name required, the rest filed and forgotten. A folded document passes between hands — harbor authority, ink already bled once. Names in columns, most crossed out. Yours is not yet there. The pen sits warm in your grip. The notebook holds pages you have not yet written on. What do you write?

## Turn 3

The figure steps closer without moving — the fog shifts and they are simply nearer, as though distance were a courtesy the grey performs rather than a fact geometry requires. Your pen touches the page and writes something without your meaning it to: a name you carried here from before the fog, from when stone was the only language you trusted. The figure watches the page fill and does not remark on it. They reach into their coat and produce a second document, this one already creased, already bleeding ink at the edges. They hold it out. The column below the name is empty. There is a line for what waits.

## Turn 4

The figure's hand holds the document level, the ink still wet where your name has settled into it. The column below is empty — not blank, but waiting, the paper faintly indented where something should go that the fog has not yet spoken. The fog thins toward the harbor's edge and thickens again toward the settlement's interior, the stone walls catching light that has no source. Your pen is in your hand. The page is full of what you did not choose to write.
