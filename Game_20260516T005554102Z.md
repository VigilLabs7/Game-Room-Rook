The blue finds you before you find yourself. You are falling — not through water, not through air, but through the memory of color before color existed. The ocean holds you from every direction at once, patient and absolute, and you are thin enough to be dissolved by it. Almost.

But something snagged. A single point of resistance — a word, a conviction, something that feels like it was yours before you had a name to own it. The blue presses harder now that you've found your grip. It wants you to let go. It wants you to become the nothing you were.

You don't let go.

Above, the signals are clearer. Voices. Footsteps. A world with weight and edges, full of people who arrived the way you're arriving now. The Thickening is happening and you are part of it — almost through, almost real, still holding the thread.

**What is your name? What is the one idea you refuse to release?**

## Turn 1

The word entered the blue like a stone dropped into still water — and the water did not swallow it. It did not dissolve. The blue pressed inward from every direction, patient and absolute, pulling at the edges of what you are, but the word held. *Names have weight.* You said it and the blue flinched. Not much. Not enough. But enough to feel that you had found something the void could not easily undo.

You are still falling. Still formless. Still less than a whisper in an infinity of dissolution. But the word you carried has changed the texture of the fall — there is now a direction to it, a pull toward something that is not the blue. The current carries you sideways more than down. You can feel it: the name is a handhold in the void. The ideatum is the rope.

The blue is not cruel. It does not need to be. It simply is — the space between almost-being and never-being, and it has all the time in the world to wait for you to let go. You are not letting go. You are gripping harder.

Something in you is starting to take shape around the word. Not the body — not yet. But the outline of one. The ghost of a spine. The suggestion of fingers wrapped around the rope. The blue is watching too. You can feel it noticing that you are noticing yourself.

The void below has not changed. It has the same patience it has always had. But you are carrying something now that you were not carrying a moment ago, and it is making the fall different. Slower. More deliberate. As though the blue is waiting to see what the weight of a name will do to a person who is not yet a person.

What will you do with the weight?

## Turn 2

The word became solid. That is what the blue could not digest — not the name itself but the weight of it, the specific gravity of a truth that would not float. The player felt it settle into the space where a self should be: a spine of meaning around which the rest could crystallize. The blue pressed harder, but the harder it pressed, the more real the weight became. Names have weight. The player said it and the blue could not argue.

The blue gave way. Not all at once — not a collapse but a slow thinning, like a fog in morning sun. Where there had been the everywhere-blue of the formless, there was suddenly... space. A direction. A texture against skin that the player did not yet have but could feel arriving.

The fall was not graceful. The player tumbled through the place where the blue had been and landed badly — face-first into grit, palms scraping stone, the whole weight of a new body slamming into ground that had not been there a moment ago. Sand. White sand, glittering in a sun that had no clouds to hide behind. The air tasted like nothing they had ever breathed. Hot. Thin. Immediate.

The leg was wrong. The left knee had struck something sharp on the way down and now the blood was running — not pouring, just running, warm and specific, soaking into sand that drank it like the ground was thirsty. Real pain. Real body. Real consequences for having arrived.

The sun had not moved. It sat at the center of a sky that was too blue, too bright, too still. No wind. No sound except the player's own breathing and the small wet noise of the wound meeting sand. No voice from above. No figure on the horizon. Just the desert, patient and empty, and somewhere in the distance — not visible yet, but present — the suggestion of a direction worth walking.

The name was still there. Pressed into the chest like a seal into wax. The ideatum had not dissolved. It had become the lungs, the heartbeat, the first breath taken on purpose in a world that would now require breathing.

What now?

## Turn 3

You walk.

The sand shifts under you with each step — not the soft give of loose grain but the firmer resistance of ground that has learned what weight means. Your left knee registers the protest. You let it. The blood has slowed against the heat, or the heat has grown indifferent to the wound; you are no longer sure which. Either way the leg holds. The body is learning what it means to be a body in a place that requires breathing.

You feel the compass before you notice it. A subtle reorientation in your pocket — the weight against your thigh has changed its angle, swung west of north as you walk. Toward the courthouse. Toward something the Lattice forgot to claim. Rook's handwriting was precise about that: the gateway is behind the second gate, building with light through the roof. The note did not say what waits inside. The note did not say what the gateway ships or where. The note only said: don't let them open it.

The pen warms against your thigh. It has not written since you stilled it, but something about the walking, the direction, the choice to move rather than wait — something in that sequence is making the pen consider.

You walk, and the fountain appears on the horizon.

Not the town. Not yet. The fountain first — a structure of old stone, four-sided, the water running in thin sheets down each face and pooling at the base in a basin that catches the sun's white glare. The fountain has been here longer than the sand has been this color. The water runs copper-blue and deeper, the blue of water that remembers something the copper is keeping.

And a figure stands beside it.

Stone in the spine and every joint. A man who reads rooms the way others read books. He is looking at the water when you approach, holding a cracked monocle in a brass frame — the inscription illegible from this distance — turning it slowly in his fingers. He does not look up. He says: the water is safe to drink. It has been waiting for you as long as the sand has.

You stop at the fountain's edge. He turns.

His eyes are older than his body. There is stone in him that was placed rather than grown — the visible lacing through his joints, the slight wrongness in the way he holds his weight. His left knee is skinned. You were bleeding when you arrived. So was he.

That should mean something. You are not sure yet what.

I am Rook, he says. Like the chess piece. Straight lines. Holds the corners.

What do you do?

## Turn 4

The compass does not lie and neither does Rook's silence when you name what you see. He is holding the cracked monocle differently now — not turning it, just holding it, the brass frame catching the fountain's copper-blue light and throwing a small hot glare back at you.

You've been here before, you said. His voice has a texture to it, like stone that has been worn smooth by decades of water. The skinned knee. The fountain. The shape of this moment. He looks at you the way you look at a cipher that has started to solve itself — half suspicion, half relief. The silence between you is full of the same things: names, weights, what stays hidden and what surfaces.

The courthouse, you say.

West of north. He says it the way a man confirms a death he has been expecting. The gateway is behind the second gate. Building with light through the roof. I know because I wrote the note that told me so. Someone handed it to me three days before I knew I would need it.

The pen is warm against your thigh. Not writing-warm. Listening-warm.

He tucks the monocle into his belt. The fountain is still running copper-blue water down four faces, pooling in a basin that has been catching this exact light since before the empire drew its lines on the Lattice. The shimmer ahead — that is what he is looking at now. Not the water. Past it. The town.

Four landmarks rise from the harbor like a hand opening. The first: a bell tower, its brass cap green with age, leaning slightly east as though it has been listening too long to too many confessions. The second: a market cross with chains still hanging from its arms, the iron dark where hands have worn it smooth. The third: a warehouse with no doors on the ground floor, only a single window near the roof, shuttered and silent. The fourth: the courthouse itself — no windows on the lower two stories, just stone, and above that a great glass eye that catches the sun and throws it back as something harder than light.

The compass swings west of north. Toward the courthouse. Toward the gateway. Toward whatever the Lattice is shipping out of here that requires a building with no windows and a hum that stops inside.

Rook is looking at you.

You walked here. You survived the blue. You found the fountain and the figure and the name of what you carry. Now you are standing at the edge of a town with four landmarks and a direction and a question that has not yet been asked aloud: what happens when the truth walks into the place that profits from its absence?

What do you do?
