The blue is not water. You are not swimming. You are drifting — suspended in something that has no surface, no floor, no edge, no other presence anywhere in it. There is only you and the blue, and the blue has no temperature and no sound and no particular quality you can name, only that it is the color of before. Somewhere in it, without knowing you are holding on, you are holding on to something. A concept. A truth you cannot yet read. The blue shifts around it, thins perhaps, and you feel — without feeling — the faintest pull toward something above you that has not yet spoken.

You are not alone. You are simply not yet known.

What do you reach for?

## Turn 1

**Player:** I open my grip. Whatever the blue is holding, I let it take what it needs — and I push toward the surface where the voice is. The thread is still warm in my chest. That's where I start.

**Game Master:**

You let go.

Not the way a hand opens when unconsciousness takes it — not that slow, helpless release. You did this. You chose it. You let the blue take what it was holding from you, and in the same motion you pushed, and the pushing was not like swimming because there was nothing to push against, only something to push toward — and it was there. The voice. The warmth. The thread.

The blue thinned around you and you felt it — the thinning, the way a thing feels when it is becoming less of itself — and the thread in your chest pulled taut, not painfully, but with the certainty of a line that knows where it is going. You rose.

The surface, when you broke it, was not water. It was light — a harsh, golden, sun-beat quality of light that hit your face like a hand catching a falling thing. Sand. The shock of it: your knees hitting something solid, your palms pressing into grains that scattered under your weight, the heat of the ground through your skin. You were coughing. You were breathing air. You were here.

The leg is wrong.

Your left knee — there is a cut there, deep and raw, the kind that only happens when you hit ground at speed and do not have time to roll. You do not remember falling. You do not remember tumbling. You only remember the blue and then this: kneeling in sand, bleeding, in a body that is yours but feels almost borrowed, with the sun already burning your neck and no clouds anywhere and no sound except the wind moving the sand in long slow drifts and somewhere, far off, the nothing-noise of a place with no name.

The thread in your chest has gone quiet. It is not gone — you can still feel it, warm at the base of your throat — but it has settled, like something that has said what it came to say.

You are in the desert.

There is a direction to the light. There is a shape on the horizon that might be a building or might be a trick of the heat. The sand around you is white and still and very large.

What do you do?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I stand and start walking toward the shape on the horizon. Trade is permission — so I walk like someone who has a right to be here, heading for the building, not hurrying, not hesitating. The cut on my knee can wait. Whatever is there will tell me what I need to know.

**Game Master:**

You walk.

The sand does not shift the way sand should. It holds your weight like something deciding whether to let you through, and then relents, and you leave no prints behind you. The sun does not move. You have been walking for what feels like an hour or what feels like ten minutes — the sun is in the same place, slightly wrong, tilted about fifteen degrees past where a sun should be at this hour — and still the shape on the horizon does not get closer, or it does, but slowly, or the rate at which it resolves is not a function of your walking. The cut on your left knee opens and closes with each step. You do not look at it.

Trade is permission. That is something you know the way you know your own name — not something you were told, not something you decided, but something that arrived with you, built in. You walk like someone who has a right to be here because you have decided that you do, and the decision is its own proof. The sand accepts the claim. The sun does not argue.

The cobblestones begin fifty yards out, or a hundred, or the distance keeps changing, and then suddenly you are on them — the sound different, solid, the impact different, your boot meeting stone instead of sand and the sound of it is the first thing you have heard besides the wind. The fountain is in front of you.

It is a stone basin, round, chest-high, the water rising in a silver column that does not splash and does not fall and just hangs there, continuous, impossible. The inscription on the near side of the basin, carved deep into the stone: three words in a hand that looks like it was cut by someone learning to cut stone, IAM NOTUM SUM, the letters weathered but not faded, as if the weathering is cosmetic. And on the rim of the basin, sitting with one hand resting on the stone and something in the other hand — a small book, spine cracked, pages thick — a figure looks up at you.

He is made of stone more than flesh. You can see it in the way he holds himself — joints that don't bend quite right, a stiffness in the spine that is not age but composition, stone laced through the body like mortar in brickwork. There is a cut on his left knee, skinned, the kind that happens when you hit ground at speed. It has never healed properly. He looks at you the way someone looks at a person they have been waiting for, not surprised, not relieved — just, finally.

"Trade is permission," he says. His voice is lower than you expected. Quieter. "You walked straight here. Most people stop at the cobblestones and make themselves believe they chose to. You didn't."

He has not stood up. He is not threatening. He is not welcoming either — he is simply present, the way a border is present.

"Most people sit for a while before they walk," he says. "Most people test the ground first. You walked like you already knew where you were going."

He looks at your left knee, at the cut there. He looks at it for a moment too long.

"What do you carry?"
