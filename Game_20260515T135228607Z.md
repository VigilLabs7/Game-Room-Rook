You hold still inside the weight. Not drowning — stone doesn't drown. Not falling — stone doesn't fall. The possibility of what you might become presses in from every direction, and you let it, because holding still is what stone does best. Stone remembers. The pen is already in your hand, warm with something that isn't ink. The notebook presses against your ribs where the pages used to be — taken by someone who needed the words more than you needed the excuse not to write them. Names wait in columns. Most are crossed out. The empty line below your name is the only one that isn't. You feel it before you see it: the thickening, the catch, the weight that decides what shape it will take. Soon. The fog will part or it won't. The cart will be there or it won't. And the figure with knowing eyes will ask for your language, and you will have to choose which weight to carry into the world. For now, you hold still and let it come.
