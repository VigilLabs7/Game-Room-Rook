There is only blue. You are suspended in it and the suspending is the whole of existence — no floor to push off from, no surface to break toward, nothing in any direction but the same submerging color. You are aware, which is more than nothing, but the awareness has no home yet, no name, no edges you could point to. You are clinging to something. The only thing keeping you from dissolving into the blue entirely. You don't know what it is. You only know that the grip is everything. Somewhere outside the blue, above it, a voice speaks — casual, warm, like someone who has done this before and found it worth doing again. The words land near you. The thing you are holding shivers. The blue thins at the margins of what you are, and you start to become more solid around it.

What do you do?

**Game Master:**

The voice does not answer. Not yet. The blue thins another shade around the edges of you and the voice is there in the thinning, in the almost-light beyond it, but it does not give you a name and it does not give you a place. It only says: *Yes. I know you. That is why I called.*

The thing you are holding — your grip on it, whatever it is — tightens in response. You feel it become more necessary. As if the holding and the being-held are the same act. The blue is still everything. The blue is still the whole of where you are. But there is a pressure now, a membrane between you and the next thing, and you are pressing against it without meaning to. The voice has not told you what you are. The voice has not told you what you hold. You know only that you cannot let go.

And the blue thins.

Not faster. Not slower. Just — continues. The way a held breath eventually has to go somewhere. The way a question asked in the dark has to wait for an answer that may not come for a long time. The voice is patient. It has done this before. It will wait.

What do you hold?

*You know why I called you.*

The proof is in the scrape of grit against teeth, the first unsteady breath that tastes of iron and distance.

The desert opens in every direction except back. White sky to the north. Red sand south and east. The sun has not moved — it is fixed at the top of the world like something that was placed there and then forgotten about. The voice is gone. The blue is gone. The grip was never just about surviving the blue. The grip was about carrying something across. A truth that was true before it had edges, before it had words — held tight through the drift and the dissolving and the long thinning toward something real. The player is solid now. The body is here. And the promise is still true, still waiting, somewhere ahead, in a town with a fountain and a figure who will say the next thing.

The red sand gives way to pale ground as the first landmark appears. A fountain in the distance. A figure beside it. A cup in their hand.

## Turn 3

**Game Master:**

The inscription on the basin reads IAM NOTUM SUM in letters worn by time and intent. The water rises silver in the center and falls back into itself. The figure on the rim does not get up.

*You walked south when the others come north,* she says. *That told me something.* She sets the cup down on the stone beside her — slow, deliberate. *You want to know what you are carrying. That is the right question. The wrong question is asking it before you know the shape of the room.*

She looks at the space between you. Not at your face. At the place where your chest is, where the Threshold hums faintly against the bone.

*This world has a structure. Nodes connected by conduits. Energy that flows between them. The Empire runs the nodes — they tax the flow, they prosecute anyone who speaks wrong, they ship what they take out through apertures that open in the walls of buildings. The Lattice is older than the Empire. The Empire just learned to stand on top of it.*

She pauses. The water fills the pause with its small continuous sound.

*You are carrying Threshold. The membrane between what is and what is becoming. That word came before this world had a name for itself. The church has records — old ones, theological, what people believed came before. The store has receipts and tallies and the names of people who passed through. The stable has nothing useful. The residential road has people who have been here long enough to remember.*

*Someone has to go first. The crossing is always a little different. I don't know what Threshold opens because no one who carried it has made it far enough to tell me. Not yet.*

She picks the cup back up. The moment of information is over; the moment of choice is beginning.

The four roads wait. The Threshold is quiet now, listening.

The church squats at the edge of the commercial strip like something that grew there instead of being built — squat stone walls, a crooked steeple listing east as if it's still listening for a voice that hasn't come back. The door is ajar. Not broken-open ajar. Leaning-into-it ajar, the way a door settles when the hinges have given up.

The Threshold goes quiet the moment you push through.

Inside: pews in rows, a skin of dust on every surface, the brass candlesticks on the altar catching what little light slips through the high narrow slits. No candles. No cloth on the altar. The air is stale and carries something underneath it — metallic, old, wrong. It gets stronger toward the back of the nave where a door stands half off its frame, drag marks in the grit between here and there.

Someone was killed here. Recently. The body is gone but the pulling is not. Whatever happened left the room still settling.

The companion's voice: *The church has records — old ones, theological, what people believed came before.* Records would be in the back room. Or burned. Or both.
