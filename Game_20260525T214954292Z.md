There is blue, and you are in it, and the having-of-a-self is the only thing that is true. Not a body yet — the suggestion of one, the memory of having edges, of being a someone instead of a somewhere. The blue has no current you can feel. You are drifting the way a thought drifts before it lands.

Something catches.

Not a handhold — a concept. A single clear thing that is yours and yours only, surfacing from beneath the formlessness like the tip of something vast. You don't know its name yet but you know it matters. You hold it the way the blue holds itself: without effort, without edge.

Then: a voice, from somewhere that is not here. *Come up.* And the concept hears it. And you begin.

Sand, now. Sun. A body that fits. Tell me what you found on the way up.

Sand runs through the fingers you are still learning are yours. The sun is direct and uninterested. You are alive in the way arrivals are alive: barely, shaped but not yet sure of your shape, the warmth still settling in your chest like something you have always carried but have not yet claimed.

You asked who came before you. The desert does not answer questions like that. It has been empty longer than memory serves, and it keeps what it has. The voice that said *come up* came from behind you, and this is wrong — voices come from above — but the wrongness will make sense later or it will not, and either way you are here.

The shape of a door that opens inward. That is what surfaced. Not a name yet, not a word, but the architecture of something that knows how to receive. You are still taking form. The concept is still yours.

What do you do with it?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I hold the shape of the door. It opens inward and I can feel it — the pull of something on the other side that has been waiting. I turn toward the voice. *Tell me, traveler — have you called others up before me?* I wait. The sand stills.

The voice does not answer. The sentinel above shifts its circuit — stone grinding faintly against air — but the sound is not language. You are being watched. You are not being spoken to. The desert makes its own accommodations.

You walk. The sand compresses differently now under your heels — fewer giving inches, more resistance — and when you look down you see why: the grains are darker here, dampened by something beneath them, or by the proximity of something ahead. A seam appears in the sand ahead, a clean line where the desert stops and something else begins. You walk toward it.

The footprints are the first proof. Not yours — older, pressed deeper, the edges softened by wind but not yet erased. Whoever came before you walked this same path. The footprints end where the cobblestones start.

The cobblestones are warm. The sun has been at them long enough to teach them heat, and they have held it the way old things hold warmth — reluctantly, then completely. The fountain is visible now. Small at this distance, then less small, then a shape that resolves into a rim and a figure on it.

The figure has one knee drawn up. The figure is watching the path you came from. The figure has been watching long enough that the watching is habit, and the habit is comfortable, and the comfort shows in the easy angle of the drawn knee and the loose hang of an arm over it.

The sentinel above has stopped tracking you. It is tracking the figure at the fountain now, the way a bird tracks something it has already decided not to take.

You step onto the cobblestones. The sound changes — sand muffled, then absent, replaced by the faint click of stone underfoot. The figure at the fountain turns its head toward you. Not quickly. Not with surprise. With the unhurried recognition of someone who has been waiting for someone and is now being proven right.

*Took you long enough,* the figure says. *I had bets with myself about which way you'd come.* A pause. *I lost. Sit.*

The stone beside the fountain is sun-warm and smooth-worn. You notice the figure's hands when it gestures — calloused in a pattern that is familiar without being named, the way a language is familiar when you have heard it spoken but never learned it yourself.

*You asked who came before,* the figure says, before you sit. *Everyone asks that. The answer is: yes. The answer to what they left is different every time. Sit. You'll find out.*
