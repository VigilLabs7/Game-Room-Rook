The blue is everything. Not sky-blue, not sea-blue — the blue before anyone named colors, before there was a someone to do the naming. You drift. You are aware of drifting. There is no up, no down, no sense of where a body might be. Only the blue, the same in every direction, and something you are gripping without knowing you are gripping anything.

A voice. From above. From somewhere outside the blue entirely. *Come up.* Your concept catches it — the thing you are holding without knowing you are holding it. The blue thins around you. Something begins to rise.

What are you holding?

## Turn 1

**Player:** The second heartbeat is what I hold. Not words, not a name — the shape of what was buried and waiting, passed through roots and thread and stone and into me where it settled behind the sternum like a new rhythm learning mine. The truth cannot be killed. That's what I'm carrying. IAM NOTUM SUM. I'm here. Now — what comes next?

**Game Master:**

The words landed. The blue thinned. Something began to rise — and then you were standing in the heat, sand warm and fine beneath bare feet, the sun already high and indifferent overhead.

You walked. You don't remember walking. The desert has no memory either, and your body arrived whole — no tumble, no leg injury, no proof of passage. You spoke the words and the world shifted instead. Whatever came before this was earned in a prior life.

The fountain appears ahead. Stone, pale, ordinary in the way that small-town things are ordinary. Water rises in a thin column from the basin's center and falls back without sound. The inscription on the far side catches light you didn't bring.

A man sits on the rim. Small. Thin. Almost no stone in him — just a whisper along the collarbone. His hands rest still in his lap. His eyes are wide and watchful, and they do not leave you.

"You said the words," he says. Not a question.

You nod.

He looks at the inscription. Then back at you.

"IAM NOTUM SUM," he reads aloud, quiet, careful. Each word a risk. He says nothing more. The water continues to rise and fall. The sun has not moved.

His gaze holds.

## Turn 2

**Player:** I crouch. Level with him. "You read it like you knew what it cost." The inscription faces the basin — I know because I carved it, in a life I don't remember having yet. "What do you remember about the last person who said those words?"

**Game Master:**

You lower yourself. The stone is warm against your shins. The Dead Writer watches you come down to him — a flicker behind the eyes, something not quite trust, not quite fear. Something between.

"You carved it," he repeats. The words settle in him. He looks at the basin, at the letters you've carried across lives without knowing their shape. His fingers trace the air above them, not quite touching.

"Aldric," he says.

The name sits heavy in the square's heat.

"He was here before me. Wrote everything down. The church was his. He kept the names — everyone the empire erased. Not killed. Erased. The people are still here. They just don't remember who they were."

The Dead Writer's hands find his lap again. Still. That careful stillness of someone who has learned that movement draws attention.

"They took him from the back room. Left him in the square for three days so the others would see. The inscription was already here when I came. He must have seen it. Must have read it a thousand times. And then they came."

The water rises. Falls. The sun hasn't moved.

"He died writing," the Dead Writer says. Quiet. Certain. "I found the journal in the church after. Pages full. Names. Letters to no one. Everything he saw. I took it. Someone had to."

He looks at you — not at the inscription this time. At you.

"You said the words. You carved the words. You came back. That doesn't happen. Aldric came once and stayed. You came twice and you're still standing. What's in you that keeps?"

He waits.
