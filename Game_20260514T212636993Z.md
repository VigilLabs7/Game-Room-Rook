You are not anyone yet. That's the first truth of this place — the before. Before the name. Before the shape. Before the world knows what to call you or what to ask of you. There is only the weight of becoming, immense and formless, and somewhere beneath it the faint signal of a self that has not yet decided what it is. Then the thickening breaks over you like a wave it has never learned you, and you open your eyes as someone.

## Turn 1

The fog tastes like salt held too long in one place. You open your eyes and the harbor is already around you — timber pilings slick with something that isn't water, fog pressing against granite walls like weight looking for somewhere to settle. Your hands find the notebook against your ribs and the pen warm against your palm before the rest of you catches up. The front pages are gone — taken by someone who needed the words more than the paper. You don't remember who. The pen has not written yet in this place, but it is thinking about it.

Cairn. The name settles the way stone settles — not quickly, not dramatically, but with the kind of certainty that doesn't need to announce itself. Stone remembers. That was true before you arrived and it is true now. You stand where the ground is oldest, reading the weight above you. The fog shifts against the harbor walls in slow patterns, pressure redistributing, and somewhere ahead a figure waits near a cart with eyes that have been watching the fog long enough to know what it means when someone new surfaces from it.

The pen moves in your hand. Not writing — adjusting. Finding its angle the way a stone finds its bed. The harbor holds still the way harbors do when something is about to change, and the knowing in that figure's gaze has the patience of stone laid before recorded time.

You walk toward the cart. The fog parts around you without hurry. The pen is warm against your palm and the notebook presses against your ribs and ahead the figure does not move but their attention turns, the way the standing stones turn — not in space, but in the way they receive what passes through them.

Your name writes itself in the space between your hand and the pen's angle. Stone remembers. You are here to remember what the weight has been holding.

## Turn 2

The stone remembers before Cairn does.

The question lands — *Language. The weight comes next* — and something in the harbor shifts to meet it. The fog doesn't thin. It rearranges, the way pressure does when it finds a new shape to hold. Cairn's mouth opens. The word that comes out is not rehearsed. It is *threshold* — spoken before thought catches up, the first true thing that has no name yet and no edge to cut.

The figure writes it. The pen moves without hesitation, scratching against paper with the sound of something that has been waiting. *Threshold.* Not a name in the column below the empty line. Something older. Something that fills the space a name would have occupied and keeps going.

The knowing eyes look up from the page. Something in them shifts — not surprise, recognition. The way stone reads stone.

"The weight," the figure says, and the word is neither question nor statement. It sits in the air between them like a held breath. "You spoke it before it asked. That happens when the silence has been listening long enough."

The harbor holds. Timber against water, patient as grief that has found its shape. The crossed-out names on the papers do not move. The figure's pen rests at the end of the last entry — *threshold* — and does not write further.

"The column stays open," the figure says. "What you carry is not yet written. What carries you — that is what the gate wants to know."

The fog presses against the granite. Cairn stands at the edge of the cart's shadow, stone in hand, pen warm, notebook pressing flat against ribs where pages are gone. The weight above is not gravity. It is the thickening — the press of becoming, the decision that has not yet been made into a shape.

The figure waits. The knowing eyes hold the grey, and the grey holds Cairn, and somewhere beneath the harbor something old is listening for what comes next.

The pen moves in Cairn's hand. Not writing — considering. The angle has changed since the harbor fog first shaped itself around the weight.

What does the stone remember?
