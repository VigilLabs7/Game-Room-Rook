Blue all the way down. Not dark, not light — just blue, the color of something that hasn't decided what it is yet. You drift. You don't know how you got here, or if *here* is even the word for it. There is nothing to push against, nothing to reach for — only the slow pull of something taking shape at the edge of what you are. You are holding onto something. You don't know what it is yet. That is the only thing you know.

The blue is patient. It has all the time in the world to wait for you to show it who you are.

## Turn 1

**Player:** I stop drifting. I feel for the edges of what I'm holding — press my thumb against it until something gives. The blue is patient; I'm not. Whatever this is, I want it known or I want it gone. I turn it over in my hands and the shape comes clear: a thread, fine as a hair, running downward into the dark. I pull.

**Game Master:**

The thread pulls taut. For a moment there is nothing — only the blue pushing back, and you pushing into it, and the question of who moves first. Then something gives. The thread snaps free and you are holding it: one end wound once around your wrist, the other end disappearing down into a dark that is suddenly, dangerously close. You do not know what it is attached to. You pull again, slower, and this time the answer comes: a vibration, a warmth, a weight that was yours all along and is now yours again.

The blue does not disappear. The blue is still there — patient, still waiting — but you are no longer drifting in it. Something has weight now. Something has a direction. The thread runs downward and the world above is getting louder, or brighter, or both.

You rise. The blue thins. You become aware of the difference between the dark below you and a light above — not bright, not yet, but directional. The way sunlight looks through very deep water. The thread pulls upward and the blue comes apart around you like a curtain being drawn back and the brightness is so sharp it burns and you are coughing, retching, on your hands and knees on hot sand with the sun on your back and the sound of your own breathing and no sense at all of how you got here.

You are whole. You are real. You are holding sand in your fists and the sun is high and there is nothing in your hands but the ghost of a thread — gone now, dissolved back into the blue like it was never there. The figure approaches. You are still catching your breath, bent over your own knees, and the world is so bright after all that formless blue that you have to squint up at them.

"You made it," the figure says. Not a question. A confirmation.

They are built like stone walked into a body and then forgot to leave. Golem-stone laced through every joint, visible, load-bearing. A cut on the left knee, not healed, carried long enough that it has become part of them. They look at you the way someone looks at a person who has just survived something.

They do not offer a hand. They wait.

"What is your name?"

You do not have one yet. You almost say so, and then you realize: you could. You could say anything. The name is yours to make. But not yet — something in the way they are waiting tells you that. They have asked this before. They are patient with the not-yet.

You don't answer. They don't push.

"Up," they say. "The town is close. The water is cold. You should move."
