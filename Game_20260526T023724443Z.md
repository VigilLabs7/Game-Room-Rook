"Tell me the word," he says, "and I will write what you give me. Exactly. Letter for letter. The way you say it. Not what you think it means — you have already told me that. Just the word."

The word flares. Not hot — present. Aware. The way a held breath is aware of the lungs that are holding it.

Behind you, from the back room, a sound. Not a word. Not paper. The sound of someone standing up from a chair — the scrape of wood on stone, sudden and deliberate. The Dead Writer is on their feet. You did not hear them rise. You did not hear them move to the doorway.

The officer's eyes move past you to the back room. Something crosses his face that is not surprise and not fear but something that lives in the space between them — the expression of a man who has been expecting something and dreading the moment it arrives.

"Writer," he says. Not to you. To whoever is standing in the doorway behind you. "This is not your room."

"It is," the Dead Writer says. Their voice is quieter than you remember — thinner, the sound of someone who has been writing for so long they have almost talked themselves out of words. "It has always been my room. You just didn't know you were borrowing it."

The officer's pen lowers. Not a surrender — a pause. He is looking at the doorway behind you with an expression that has moved past patience and into something more complicated. The ledger is still open. The page is still waiting.

The word burns. The officer and the Writer are looking at each other around you. Whatever this is, you are standing in the middle of it.
